## Dissertation Papers

The three papers that comprise my dissertation, *Administrative Architecture and Medicaid: Essays on Barriers, Burdens, and Bargaining*.

### [Fear as a Barrier to Coverage: The Chilling Effect of the Public Charge Rule on Children's Medicaid Enrollment](public-charge-chilling.pdf)

When the Trump administration expanded the "public charge" rule in 2019, did Medicaid-eligible citizen children in immigrant families drop coverage out of fear, even though they were formally exempt from the rule? Using American Community Survey data on 1,622,582 Medicaid-eligible U.S. citizen children below 200% of the federal poverty level (2016–2024) and restricted-access California Health Interview Survey data on 220,925 adults (2015–2024, four-level decomposition: U.S.-born, naturalized, green-card holder, no green card), I estimate difference-in-differences and event-study models comparing citizen children in mixed-status households with those in citizen-only households. Citizen children in mixed-status families experienced a 2.0-percentage-point decline in Medicaid enrollment after the September 2018 public-charge announcement (*p* < 0.001), with no pre-policy divergence in the event-study leads. The chill is concentrated in mixed-status families with U.S.-citizen children, attenuated in fully documented households, and intensifies with county-level immigration-enforcement activity — evidence that the rule operated through fear rather than through legal eligibility changes.

### [Suspended, Not Terminated: Medicaid Coverage Continuity Across the Prison Door](suspension-termination.pdf)

When someone enters prison, most states terminate their Medicaid; a growing minority suspend it instead, so coverage automatically reactivates at release. Using the staggered rollout of state suspension policies between 2005 and 2022 and linking a newly-constructed state-year policy panel (triangulated from six independent sources) to the Census Bureau's Criminal Justice Administrative Records System (CJARS) Justice Outcomes Explorer across 31 states, I estimate the intent-to-treat association between suspension policies and post-release Medicaid enrollment, employment, earnings, and mortality. The primary outcome is point-in-time Medicaid enrollment at one, three, and five years post-release. Suspension states show meaningful Medicaid-continuity advantages in the immediate post-release window — when reentry mortality risk peaks — with effects on employment and earnings consistent with reduced administrative friction at a moment of acute vulnerability.

### [Pooling Is Not Enough: Medicaid Drug Purchasing Pools, Pharmacy-Benefit Governance, and Supplemental Rebate Capture](medicaid-drug-pools.pdf)

Thirty-three states participate in interstate Medicaid drug purchasing pools (SSDC, NMPI, TOP), but whether pooling itself raises supplemental rebate capture is unclear because states often join pools alongside broader pharmacy-benefit governance reforms — uniform preferred drug lists, pharmacy carve-outs, rebate-administration changes. I construct a 50-state, 2002–2024 panel linking CMS-64 supplemental rebate data to a new spell-coded pharmacy-benefit governance dataset and estimate heterogeneity-robust dynamic event-study models. Pool membership alone does essentially nothing: the any-pool standalone effect is 0.06 percentage points (*p* = 0.95), and the later-adopter SSDC benchmark of 3.37 percentage points falls to 1.28 percentage points after governance controls. Gains concentrate in states that simultaneously pool, run a uniform preferred drug list, and carve pharmacy out of managed care — a 2.22-percentage-point dynamic increase from a 1.75-percentage-point pre-bundle baseline, or roughly a 127 percent relative gain, cross-validated across four canonical R-package implementations of staggered-DiD estimators. State-level Black-share heterogeneity shows the bundled positive is not concentrated in any single demographic-composition band.

## Manuscripts in Review or Prepared for Submission

Single-author papers prepared for *Health Affairs* and *Health Affairs Scholar*, ordered by my assessment of academic strength.

### [Removing the Gate: Medicaid Prior Authorization for Buprenorphine and Prescribing](buprenorphine-pa.pdf)

Buprenorphine is a first-line treatment for opioid use disorder, but most state Medicaid programs historically required prior authorization before pharmacies could dispense it. Between 2015 and 2022, twelve states removed Medicaid PA for buprenorphine. I use the staggered rollout and a difference-in-differences design on State Drug Utilization Data to test whether the bureaucratic barrier was constraining access. Removal states see precisely-estimated increases in Medicaid buprenorphine prescribing concentrated in primary-care and rural prescribers; the policy-relevant magnitude is informative for the federal removal of x-waiver and ongoing PA requirements, and is smaller than the largest advocacy estimates would predict.

### [Watching the Workers: Electronic Visit Verification and the Medicaid Home Care Market](evv-rollout.pdf)

The 21st Century Cures Act required state Medicaid programs to install Electronic Visit Verification (EVV) systems for home-based personal-care services, with the Congressional Budget Office projecting $290 million in federal savings. Using newly released T-MSIS claims data across 51 jurisdictions (2018–2024) and a staggered difference-in-differences design, I find no significant effect of EVV on personal-care quarterly spending (+$0.62 million per state-quarter, *p* = 0.974) or on total home and community-based services spending (+$2.37 million, *p* = 0.893). A mechanism decomposition finds no significant change in price per claim, claims per provider, provider counts, or beneficiary counts. The minimum detectable effect is roughly 209 times the CBO projection, so modest savings cannot be ruled out — but policymakers should not assume EVV generates large verifiable federal savings, and should prioritize implementation effectiveness, claims auditing, and service-continuity safeguards before relying on EVV for federal budget offsets.

### [Coverage Without Citizenship: California's Medi-Cal Adult Expansion at 1–138 vs. 1–200% FPL](ca-medi-cal-2024-expansion.pdf)

California's Ages 26–49 Adult Full-Scope Medi-Cal Expansion, effective January 1, 2024, extended full-benefit Medi-Cal to income-eligible residents regardless of immigration status. Building a 2018–2024 IPUMS American Community Survey state-year panel for low-income noncitizens ages 26–49 and estimating ridge-augmented synthetic control (`pysyncon`) alongside synthetic difference-in-differences (`synthdid`) against a 43-state donor pool that excludes states with comparable adult immigrant-coverage policies, I report two co-primary FPL bands: 1–200% (broad) and 1–138% (Medicaid-relevant). Across both bands and both estimators, California uninsurance is lower in 2024 than the synthetic counterfactual by 7.0 to 8.6 percentage points; the Medicaid coverage gain attenuates at the narrower 1–138% band (+1.8 pp `pysyncon`; +7.6 pp `synthdid`) but does not flip sign. Placebo-in-space and HonestDiD diagnostics are weaker than ideal (Fisher-exact rank *p* = 0.977 in all eight cells), so the headline is read as a directional public-data screen rather than a precision estimate.

## Working Papers

In rough order of my assessment of academic strength — publishability at top venues, novelty of design, robustness of identification, and substantive importance — strongest first.

### [When the Office Closes: Administrative Infrastructure, the SSI–Medicaid Linkage, and Medicaid Coverage Among Disabled Adults](ssa-office-closures-medicaid.pdf)

The Social Security Administration's field-office network is a core part of the disability-benefit system, and in most states it is also part of Medicaid administration: an SSI eligibility determination automatically confers Medicaid in "1634" states, while "209(b)" states keep more restrictive Medicaid criteria. Combining ACS and BRFSS state-year data 2005–2023 with the Deshpande–Li closure roster and a staggered difference-in-differences design, I ask whether SSA field-office closures reduced Medicaid coverage among disabled adults — and whether the effect is stronger where SSI and Medicaid are institutionally linked. The national average effect is close to zero (+0.17 pp; 95% CI [−2.39, +2.72]), but the subgroup pattern is consistent with the mechanism: Medicaid coverage falls in 1634 jurisdictions (−1.42 pp; CI [−4.51, +1.67]) and rises in 209(b) states (+2.73 pp; CI [+0.45, +5.01]). The 1634-vs-209(b) contrast is −4.15 pp (CI [−7.63, −0.68]) — evidence that the SSI on-ramp matters for Medicaid coverage in exactly the states where SSI receipt is the gateway.

### [Section 1108's Long Shadow: Sixty Years of Capped Medicaid in the U.S. Territories, 1968–2024](territorial-medicaid-caps.pdf)

Section 1108(c) has capped federal Medicaid contributions to the five U.S. territories (Puerto Rico, USVI, Guam, American Samoa, CNMI) since 1968 — the same per-capita-block-grant architecture recent state restructuring proposals would impose nationally. The historical consequences have not been quantified with modern causal-inference designs, partly because Puerto Rico vital-statistics data sit in a Spanish-language *Informe* corpus that standard federal systems do not carry. I machine-extracted the 1950–2023 *Informe* corpus into a 559-cell validated panel and ran three historical synthetic-control arms (1968 imposition, 2011 ACA Section 2005 partial uncapping, 2019 cliff) plus a prospective reverse-counterfactual that applies published Medicaid-mortality elasticities to the fifty states. The historical Puerto Rico arms produce informative nulls — read honestly as level-mismatch nulls, because Puerto Rico's 1959–1967 mean IMR of 42.7 per 1,000 sat 13.3 points above the donor-pool mean (29.4) and 3.4 points above even Mississippi (39.3), making mechanical demographic-transition convergence dominate the post-1968 trajectory. The reverse counterfactual is concrete: a 1968-scaled cap on the fifty states would remove $513 billion in federal Medicaid in FY2024 and imply 4,451 to 14,838 excess infant deaths per year under two peer-reviewed elasticities (97,032 to 323,438 cumulatively over FY1984–FY2024); Graham–Cassidy 2017 implies 529 to 1,762 annually. A modern adult-mortality sensitivity using Miller–Johnson–Wherry implies the no-cap funding gap could finance about 40,700 additional Medicaid-covered near-elderly low-income adults and avert roughly 420 adult deaths per year.

### [A House Divided: Modern Heterogeneous-Effects Estimators and the Contested Mortality Effects of Hospital Desegregation, 1959–1988](title-vi-desegregation-adjudication.pdf)

Almond, Chay, and Greenstone (2006) argued that the Title VI Medicare-certification campaign of 1966–1968 produced a large decline in Black postneonatal mortality in the rural South; Anderson, Charles, and Rees (2024) revisited the question and reported a statistically null effect. I apply five staggered difference-in-differences estimators — two-way fixed effects, Callaway–Sant'Anna, Borusyak–Jaravel–Spiess, de Chaisemartin–D'Haultfœuille, and Sun–Abraham — to a unified eleven-state Confederate-South panel of Black postneonatal mortality, and additionally construct a federal-source-uniform extended panel back to 1959 by combining NCHS Mortality Detail File microdata with a newly transcribed pre-1968 county-year-race natality denominator from state Department of Public Health annual reports and federal VSUS Vol I tabulations. On the extended 1959–1988 panel, the modern heterogeneity-robust estimators recover a positive, statistically significant mortality decline for Black infants that the TWFE-only Anderson–Charles–Rees specification fails to detect — adjudicating the disagreement by isolating which design choices drive the divergence and recovering an answer that respects both the data extension and the modern methods.

### [Coverage by State Choice: Medicaid Expansions for Undocumented Immigrants and Insurance Coverage](noncitizen-coverage.pdf)

A handful of states have used their own funds to extend Medicaid-equivalent coverage to undocumented adults. Using a state-level synthetic-control design on ACS coverage data restricted to noncitizen subsamples, I measure whether the expansions translated into measurable coverage gains for the target population. Expansion states show clear coverage gains relative to synthetic controls — concentrated in the 26–49 age band that most state expansions target — with magnitudes that confirm survey data can detect the policy despite the well-documented ACS undercount of undocumented adults. The analysis transparently bounds what public data can establish about a population partly invisible to federal surveys, and frames the noncitizen coverage gap as a tractable state-policy question.

### [State Medicaid Doula Coverage and Birth Outcomes: A Staggered Difference-in-Differences Analysis, 2019–2024](doula-coverage.pdf)

Twenty-seven states have added doula services to their Medicaid benefit packages between 2014 and 2026, motivated by persistent racial inequities in U.S. maternal and infant health. Using a state-by-year panel of all 50 states and DC for 2019–2024 from NCHS Vital Statistics Rapid Release and CDC WONDER race-by-state tabulations, with direct WONDER all-births-by-race denominators and the heterogeneity-robust Callaway–Sant'Anna estimator, I find that pooled state-year average treatment effects on the seven primary natality outcomes are small in magnitude and not consistent with clinically meaningful improvement. Race-stratified estimates do not detect the disparity-narrowing pattern in non-Hispanic Black mothers that earlier population-level analyses with constructed denominators had reported. An unexpected +3.69-percentage-point ATT on the cesarean rate for non-Hispanic American Indian and Alaska Native mothers runs opposite to the Cochrane labor-support evidence and is treated as hypothesis-generating given small cell counts. State Medicaid agencies, CMS, MACPAC, and ASPE should treat current population-level evidence as inconclusive.

### [Medicaid Expansion, Coverage Gains, and Mortality After Prison Release](post-release-mortality.pdf)

Each year, over 600,000 adults are released from U.S. state prisons into communities where they face mortality rates twelve times the general-population rate, with drug-overdose risk elevated 129-fold in the first two weeks. Using the Census Bureau's Justice Outcomes Explorer to link criminal-justice records to federal administrative data on mortality, Medicaid enrollment, and employment, I estimate the effect of ACA Medicaid expansion on post-release outcomes via a staggered difference-in-differences design keyed to release-year exposure across 23 states. The clearest finding is a one-year Medicaid first-stage of +15.9 percentage points under Local Projections Difference-in-Differences (*p* = 0.046); the corresponding mortality response at one, three, and five years post-release is an honest null whose confidence interval bounds the effect to be small. The coverage on-ramp works but does not by itself deliver the large mortality reduction the underlying public-health intuition would suggest in a short-window analysis.

### [The 1972 §1902(f) Wedge: A Fifty-Year Border-Pair Look at Medicaid Coverage, SSI Uptake, and Mortality in Eleven "209(b)" States](ssi-1972-209b-border-rd.pdf)

The 1972 federalization of SSI created a hidden split: most states tied Medicaid eligibility to SSI receipt ("1634 states"), but eleven so-called "209(b)" states kept their own, more restrictive Medicaid criteria. Using adjacent county pairs straddling 209(b)/non-209(b) state borders and a difference-in-differences design on fifty years of administrative coverage, SSI, and mortality data, I measure how the half-century-old policy choice has shaped the safety net for elderly and disabled adults at the border. 209(b) states show persistently lower SSI uptake and meaningful Medicaid coverage gaps at the border; the mortality gradient is informative but underpowered to support a definitive causal claim — a "policy chose decades ago to leave coverage on the table" finding consistent with the SSA-closures companion paper.

### [Federally Qualified Health Centers and County Uninsurance: An Association Analysis with Proper Shift-Share Inference](fqhc-nap-bartik-iv.pdf)

I estimate the association between Federally Qualified Health Center (FQHC) capacity, expanded through HRSA Health Center Program (Section 330) grants, and county-level under-65 uninsurance during the post-2016 funding plateau, using a shift-share design with proper state-clustered instrumental-variables inference on mainland US counties (n = 3,138) over fiscal years 2017–2022 (N = 15,685 county-years). FQHC capacity is built from the HRSA Uniform Data System with a corrected ZIP-to-county allocation that retains 95% of UDS sites (versus 62% in standard implementations) and the Area Health Resource File 2016–2024. The Bartik-style instrument's first stage is too weak in this setting to support causal inference, and the paper converts that finding into a methods contribution about when shift-share inference is and is not appropriate for federal grant programs — with explicit guidance on which exclusion-restriction failures are diagnosable in public data and which require restricted data not available outside HRSA.

### [Who Responds When Children's Medicaid Becomes Continuous?](continuous-eligibility-children.pdf)

Section 5112 of the Consolidated Appropriations Act 2023 required all state Medicaid and CHIP programs to provide 12 months of continuous eligibility to children under age 19 beginning January 1, 2024. Four states went further with §1115 waivers extending continuous eligibility from birth through age 5 or 6: Oregon (July 2023), Washington (April 2023), New Mexico (January 2024), and Pennsylvania (November 2024). Using the 2016–2025 CPS Annual Social and Economic Supplement with a 99.3-percent parent-child match and month-correct treatment timing from primary CMS and state-agency sources, I find the pooled effect on parents of children ages 0–6 is statistically and substantively null: weekly hours change by +0.10 (*p* = 0.786) and full-time employment by −0.6 percentage points (*p* = 0.492). Among parents whose highest credential is a high-school diploma or GED, however, weekly hours rise by +1.12 (*p* < 0.001) and full-time status by +2.8 percentage points (*p* = 0.016) — heterogeneity consistent with a credit-constrained, non-cash-time-substitution interpretation that the pooled estimate hides.

### [The Medicaid IMD Exclusion and the Mid-Century Decline of State Mental Hospitals: A Within-State Pre-Trend Test on Newly-Digitized NIMH Census Data, 1947–1972](imd-exclusion-deinstitutionalization.pdf)

Medicaid's 1965 IMD exclusion — codified at Section 1905(a)(B) — denied federal financial participation for inpatient psychiatric care delivered to adults aged 22–64 in institutions for mental diseases. The institutional historiography has blamed the post-1965 collapse of the U.S. state-mental-hospital system on this exclusion for thirty-five years, but the within-state pre-trend test on annual state-mental-hospital census data had not been published because the NIMH annual census was not machine-readable. I optically character-recognized the 1947–1968 NIMH census, built a 358-row state-year resident-patient panel, and estimated a Callaway–Sant'Anna event study across five Medicaid-adoption cohorts. The first post-Medicaid year shows a 22 percent decline in state-hospital residents per 100,000 (event-study coefficient −0.245 log-points; 95% CI [−0.418, −0.071]); a treat-age × post-Medicaid × state-adoption triple-difference on the deaths-of-despair bundle returns −7.58 per 100,000 (a 21 percent relative reduction). Federal policymakers weighing further IMD-exclusion rollback under §1115 SMI/SED waivers and the *SUPPORT Act* should anchor those choices to this baseline.

### [Rationing the Revolution: The Causal Effect of State Medicaid GLP-1 Coverage Decisions on Obesity-Indication Prescription Utilization](glp1-coverage.pdf)

Medicaid coverage of GLP-1 receptor agonists for obesity is optional under Section 1927 of the Social Security Act, while the same products are mandatorily covered for diabetes. Thirteen states covered GLP-1s for obesity as of January 2026, down from sixteen in October 2025 after California, New Hampshire, Pennsylvania, and South Carolina rolled coverage back. Using Medicaid State Drug Utilization Data 2018–2025 and a primary-source seventeen-state coverage-adoption panel, I combine staggered difference-in-differences with a within-state triple-difference comparing obesity-indication and diabetes-indication prescriptions. State coverage adoption is associated with an increase of approximately 2.4 log points in obesity-indication prescriptions per 1,000 Medicaid enrollees — a roughly tenfold increase off a near-zero pre-Wegovy baseline (*p* < 0.001 in both main and triple-difference specifications). The diabetes-indication placebo is null. The simultaneous wave of state rollbacks effective January 2026 is likely to reverse a substantial share of the 2022–2025 expansion; the federal BALANCE model offers a counterweight if states have the fiscal capacity to participate.

### [When Work Requirements Met the States: Two Case Studies of Section 1115 Medicaid Community Engagement Mandates in Arkansas and Georgia](work-requirements.pdf)

The 2025 federal budget reconciliation law requires states to impose §1115 Medicaid community-engagement requirements on expansion adults beginning in 2027. The two prior state implementations long enough for ex-post evaluation — Arkansas Works (2018–2019) and Georgia Pathways to Coverage (2023–ongoing) — are substantively opposite: Arkansas conditioned continued coverage on documented hours within an existing expansion population, while Georgia conditioned entry into a new partial expansion at 100% of poverty on the same documentation. I evaluate each as an independent case study on state-year ACS aggregates with cluster-robust, small-cluster-corrected, and randomization inference. Arkansas is consistent in sign and rough diluted magnitude with the Sommers et al. (2020) individual-panel evidence on coverage loss, with the uninsurance increase concentrating in the targeted age band and among Black enrollees, but is underpowered for an independent claim. Georgia is a descriptive null on coverage, employment, and uninsurance, consistent with realized take-up of roughly 8,000 of 345,000 projected. States and CMS should weight administrative design — not the eligibility condition — as the actionable policy lever under the 2027 federal mandate.

### [State Nursing-Home Staffing Floors and the Distributional Bite: Heterogeneity by Medicaid Share and Rurality](nh-staffing-floor-bite.pdf)

State nursing-home minimum staffing floors are again the principal regulatory lever for direct-care staffing after the federal CMS staffing rule was vacated and repealed in late 2025. Existing evaluations recover modest positive average effects but do not characterize how the response varies with the financing margins that make compliance most difficult. Using a 510,189-row CMS Payroll-Based Journal facility-quarter panel covering 2017Q1–2025Q4 and 11 analytic states adopting numerical floors between 2018Q1 and 2024Q3, I construct each facility's pre-policy "bite" (statutory floor minus baseline staffing), merge pre-treatment LTCFocus Medicaid-day shares and Care Compare rurality, and estimate a Callaway–Sant'Anna doubly-robust ATT(g,t), a continuous-bite dose-response, and a triple-interaction `bite × Medicaid-share × post`. The triple-interaction equals +0.153 hours per resident day per unit of Medicaid share (SE 0.034, *p* < 0.001). The dose-response is monotone (Q1→Q5 spread 0.37 HPRD pooled) and steepens with Medicaid share (0.32 / 0.40 / 0.43 HPRD across terciles). Rural facilities show a Q1→Q5 spread of 0.51 HPRD versus 0.34 urban. The story is that average effects mask large distributional asymmetries: facilities with higher Medicaid penetration and rural location absorb the compliance burden differently than higher-margin urban facilities.

### [State-Elected Procedural-Disenrollment Delays and Medicaid Renewal Outcomes During the 2023 Unwinding](unwind-compliance.pdf)

During the 2023–2024 Medicaid unwinding, the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services identified that many states were conducting automatic renewals at the household rather than individual level and, in some states, concurred in pauses of procedural disenrollments. Using a state-month panel of 51 jurisdictions over 18 months, I estimate difference-in-differences models exploiting the staggered timing of those pauses across 15 states. In as-treated models, pauses were associated with a 6.5-percentage-point increase in renewal completion rates (*p* = 0.05); corresponding estimates for procedural termination and total disenrollment were negative but imprecise. However, the intent-to-treat specification is null, renewal-completion event studies fail formal pre-trend tests, the headline estimate does not survive multiple-testing adjustment, and approximate power is limited. These results provide suggestive evidence that CMS-concurred pauses may have improved renewal completion during the unwinding, but they do not support strong causal claims about the effects of federal enforcement.

### [Big Money, Small Margin: State-Directed Payments in Medicaid Managed Care and Hospital Finance](state-directed-payments-panel.pdf)

State-Directed Payments (SDPs) have grown rapidly as a state Medicaid financing tool — and were targeted for restriction in the 2026 OBBBA federal budget legislation. Assembling the first longitudinal arrangement-level SDP panel and matching to hospital Medicare cost-report financial data, I estimate pre-OBBBA hospital financial effects and document the SDP arrangement structure. The pooled effect on hospital financial margins is a transparent null, with heterogeneity by provider type that suggests SDPs are not the broad hospital-rescue tool they are sometimes described as: academic medical centers absorb most of the targeted flow, while smaller community hospitals see little measurable financial benefit. The panel construction and pooled null together discipline the OBBBA-era debate over how aggressively to restrict SDPs.

### [Bans Without Bite: State Spread-Pricing Laws and Medicaid Generic Drug Spending](pbm-spread-pricing.pdf)

A wave of states have passed laws banning Pharmacy Benefit Managers (PBMs) from "spread pricing" inside Medicaid managed care. Using CMS State Drug Utilization Data, National Average Drug Acquisition Cost (NADAC), and Medicaid enrollment files across 51 jurisdictions 2016Q1–2024Q4, plus a new state-year panel coding five bundled cost-containment actions, I apply staggered difference-in-differences (Callaway–Sant'Anna and TWFE) with a triple-difference (generic vs. brand within MCO) and a pre-registered negative control (MCO vs. fee-for-service within state). The pooled effect on generic drug spending is a transparent null with confidence intervals that exclude the savings claims made in the legislative-history record. The bundled-action specification matters: states that paired spread-pricing bans with additional cost-containment actions show movement on bundled-action outcomes that single-policy advocacy attributes to the spread ban alone.

### [Setup Leaves Public Traces: A Public-Data Linkage Playbook for Medicaid Program Integrity](public-data-medicaid-fraud-playbook.pdf)

State Medicaid program-integrity teams face large provider universes, fragmented oversight responsibilities, and limited investigative capacity. Public records cannot establish Medicaid fraud by themselves, but they can improve the triage process that precedes subpoena, data-use agreement, chart review, and beneficiary-level investigation. This paper develops a public-data linkage playbook for Medicaid fraud, waste, and abuse screening. Using a New York Medicaid demonstration across six provider categories, the paper shows how public data can convert broad outlier queues into related-entity maps, signal domains, confidence-tiered linkages, and targeted restricted-data requests. In a 928-NPI home-care subset, the workflow narrowed a 441-provider public baseline queue representing $49.4 billion in payment exposure down to a 66-provider tier-3 high-confidence queue — a 6.5-fold concentration of investigative attention.

### [Heterogeneous Compliance with the FTC Express Scripts Consent Order: A Pair-Level Insulin Formulary Snapshot of the First Post-Settlement Plan Year](express-scripts-ftc-settlement.pdf)

The February 2026 Federal Trade Commission consent order against Express Scripts (Docket 9437) is the first behavioral antitrust remedy ever imposed on PBM formulary architecture. Section I prohibits Express Scripts from placing the Low-WAC version of a within-manufacturer authorized-generic insulin pair on a less favorable tier than the same-manufacturer High-WAC version, but does not bind CVS Caremark or OptumRx (both still in administrative litigation). I assemble the first publicly enumerated list of Section-I-qualifying within-manufacturer authorized-generic insulin pairs, ingest the four April 1, 2026 Express Scripts Standard Offering PDFs, and characterize post-Implementation-Date compliance on the four manufacturer pairs the rule reaches. Express Scripts' April 2026 National Preferred Formulary achieves tier parity on the Lilly Humalog and Viatris Semglee pairs, excludes the Novo Nordisk NovoLog franchise entirely (Section I moot), and shows a Section-I-violation-direction configuration on the Sanofi Lantus / unbranded insulin glargine pair — the first publicly observable such configuration. Rule-based behavioral antitrust enforcement of PBM formulary architecture is observable, partially effective, and amenable to public-data audit; the Sanofi configuration warrants FTC Section V monitoring.

### [Trading Inpatient Beds for Financial Lifelines: Rural Emergency Hospital Conversions and Hospital Margins](rural-emergency-hospitals.pdf)

The 2023 Rural Emergency Hospital (REH) designation lets struggling rural hospitals close their inpatient services and survive on enhanced federal payments. Using a Callaway–Sant'Anna event study on CMS-trimmed REH conversions in the first program year, I measure what happens at converting hospitals: inpatient capacity falls sharply and significantly (the mechanism, *p* < 0.001), while year-one hospital operating margins move in a positive direction but are not decisively improved (primary CS-trimmed +7.4 percentage points, *p* = 0.21). Larger sensitivities (+17.8 pp full-pool CS; +23.0 pp trimmed-pool TWFE; +29.1 pp full-pool TWFE) are reported as sensitivities, never as the headline. An operating-margin reclassification artifact (HCRIS Worksheet G-3) is preserved as a distinct measurement contribution. The publishable headline is the mechanism finding, with the financial verdict reported as honest year-one inconclusiveness.

### [Paperwork, Proof, and Pediatric Coverage: The 2006 Citizenship Documentation Rule as an Administrative Friction Shock](dra-2006-citizenship-friction.pdf)

The Deficit Reduction Act of 2005 — effective July 1, 2006 — ended self-attestation of citizenship for Medicaid enrollment and required documentary proof. This was a purely administrative shock to citizens (federal eligibility rules were unchanged) that imposed documentation friction on individuals who were already citizens but lacked ready access to birth certificates, passports, or naturalization papers. Using a difference-in-discontinuities design at the rule's effective date and a citizen-aware interrupted time series, I measure how the friction shock translated into pediatric Medicaid coverage losses. The post-rule coverage drop is concentrated in low-income citizen children whose families needed time to produce documents, with a partial recovery as state documentation infrastructure improved — consistent with friction-as-eligibility-tax rather than as a screen for ineligible enrollees.

### [Putting Teeth Into Medicaid: State Adult Dental Benefit Expansions and Population-Level Utilization](adult-dental.pdf)

I estimate whether the 2020–2023 wave of state Medicaid adult dental benefit expansions produced detectable changes in past-year dental visits, cost-related care barriers, and self-rated health at the state adult population level. Using a state-year panel (50 states + DC, 2016–2023) linking BRFSS to a state-level adult Medicaid dental benefit scope panel constructed from CareQuest Institute surveys, KFF Medicaid benefits data, and state Medicaid agency bulletins (primary-outcome analytic panel restricted to even years 2018/2020/2022 when BRFSS fielded the optional Oral Health module), the pooled treatment effect is an honest null with bounded confidence intervals. The paper transparently flags BRFSS 2024's later release as the cleanest forward power check and sets a defensible "what we currently can and cannot detect" baseline that future BRFSS releases will sharpen.

### [Coverage Effects of Oregon's Healthier Oregon All-Ages Expansion and the Co-Occurring OHP Bridge Program: Synthetic-Control Evidence from the ACS, 2018–2024](oregon-healthier-oregon-sdid.pdf)

Oregon's Healthier Oregon expansion extended full Oregon Health Plan benefits to all income-eligible residents regardless of immigration status on July 1, 2023; because the program already covered ages 0–25 and adults 55+, the newly exposed adult target band is ages 26–54. Building a 2018–2024 ACS state-year panel for low-income noncitizen adults ages 26–54 at 1–200% of the federal poverty line and estimating ridge-augmented synthetic-control gaps for Oregon versus donor states that did not adopt comparable adult immigrant-coverage policies during the window, I find that in the 2024 post year, uninsurance was 21.0% versus a synthetic counterfactual of 46.4% (−25.4 percentage points), and Medicaid or means-tested public coverage rose from 24.5% to 43.8% (+19.3 pp). Formal synthetic difference-in-differences corroborates the uninsurance gap (−24.6 pp, placebo *p* = 0.008) and attenuates the Medicaid gap (+15.3 pp, *p* = 0.17). Because the 2024 post year also contains the co-occurring Oregon Health Plan Bridge Basic Health Program that began July 1, 2024, and because the design uses one post-treatment ACS year, the estimates should be read as a directional public-data screen rather than a mature causal estimate.

### [Re-examining the OBRA Medicaid Pregnancy Expansions](obra-pregnancy-expansion-update.pdf)

The OBRA 1986/87/89/90 Medicaid pregnancy expansions are a foundational result in the Medicaid-and-mortality literature. I re-estimate the effect of those expansions on state-level infant mortality using a continuous-intensity difference-in-differences design built on the Currie–Gruber published simulated-eligibility instrument, on a 50-jurisdiction state-year panel (1980–1995) constructed from NCHS Natality public-use microdata and NCHS Linked Birth/Infant Death cohort files (1983–1991, 1995–2004). Where the original literature found large infant-mortality effects, the modern replication produces estimates statistically indistinguishable from zero. The honest-null is framed as a methodological lesson about how staggered treatment timing and TWFE contamination can amplify older estimates, with implications for how we read the rest of the foundational Medicaid-and-mortality evidence base.

### [The Procedural Wedge: How Medicaid Disenrollment Undermined Community Health Center Finances](administrative-wedge.pdf)

The 2023–2024 Medicaid unwinding disenrolled over 25 million people, approximately 69 percent for procedural reasons unrelated to eligibility. I examine whether state-level procedural disenrollment is associated with the financial health of Federally Qualified Health Centers, using a four-bin state-level pre-pandemic measure of automated renewal capacity (from the KFF/Georgetown January 2018 50-state survey, genuinely pre-treatment relative to the 2023–2024 unwinding outcome window) as a candidate instrument for procedural disenrollment. The first stage is too weak to support causal inference, so the paper is structured as a methods note on null-first-stage disclosure with a descriptive supply-side exhibit showing meaningful unwinding-period revenue stress at FQHCs in procedurally-aggressive states.

### [Fixed-Effect Sensitivity in FMAP-Kink Regression Designs: A Public-Data Methods Note on Medicaid LTSS Spending at the 50% Statutory Floor](fmap-floor-ltss-rkd.pdf)

An applied literature uses a regression kink design at the 50% Federal Medical Assistance Percentage floor to estimate state Medicaid responses to federal generosity; modern panels span the post-2014 ACA Medicaid expansion era. I characterize the public-data confidence intervals on the FMAP-kink RKD for FY2008–FY2023 and show that the drop-ACA robustness check is highly sensitive to fixed-effect residualization. Under exact two-way state and fiscal-year fixed-effect residualization, the original headline RKD result is withdrawn; the methods-note framing for fixed-effect-residualization sensitivity is the publishable contribution, with explicit guidance on what authors should disclose when using FMAP-kink RKD on public data.

### [Did Losing Medicaid Make Anyone Sicker? A Composition/Null Reading of the Post-Pandemic Unwinding in BRFSS, with Triangulating Evidence from the ACS](unwinding-adult-health.pdf)

Did the 2023 Medicaid unwinding produce detectable declines in adult coverage or adult self-reported health? Using state-month procedural-disenrollment intensity from the CMS Unwinding Data Report and Medicaid and CHIP Performance Indicators (aggregated to a 51-state cumulative procedural share over April 2023–August 2024), combined with adult outcomes from the BRFSS state-year panel (2019–2023) and ACS one-year public-use microdata (2019–2024), I run parallel difference-in-differences designs sharing a single state-intensity treatment. The composition-corrected reading is an absence of a detectable health-decline signal in publicly available state-level surveys once survey-composition shifts are accounted for — a finding that does not vindicate the unwinding so much as it disciplines what publicly available state-level surveys can and cannot establish about a policy whose adverse effects may operate through channels invisible to short-window aggregate-survey measurement.

### [Coverage Before Release: Section 1115 Medicaid Reentry Waivers and Drug Overdose Mortality](reentry-waivers.pdf)

Individuals released from incarceration face drug-overdose mortality risk 129 times that of the general population in the first two weeks post-release. In 2023, CMS began approving §1115 Reentry Demonstration waivers, allowing states to provide Medicaid-covered health services — including medications for opioid use disorder, case management, and care coordination — up to 90 days before an individual's expected release. As of late 2025, 19 states have received approval, but implementation has been staggered and slow. This paper documents the implementation timelines, the corrections-Medicaid information exchanges each state built, and the enrolled-population sizes, and frames the design space for the formal causal evaluation that will become possible as more states adopt and as the post-release outcome windows mature.

### [When Medi-Cal Recedes: Section 17000 and California's County Health Care Backstop](section-17000-backstop.pdf)

California counties retain legal responsibility for indigent residents under Welfare and Institutions Code §17000, yet that backstop faded from view after the Affordable Care Act, Medi-Cal expansion, and AB 85 redirected local indigent-care financing. The history is newly relevant because California DHCS and Legislative Analyst's Office documents indicate that federal policy changes effective October 1, 2026 and January 1, 2027 could increase county exposure to adults losing Medi-Cal. Using legal and budget materials plus a county-by-county review of official county program and health-system documents, I map California's current indigent-care landscape and the institutional pathways through which counties may absorb coverage loss. Thirty-five counties participate in the shared County Medical Services Program; among the twenty-three self-administered counties, nineteen maintain visible county-branded indigent-care programs, while four rely on county hospitals, behavioral-health systems, FQHCs, and payment-assistance structures.

### [The Fertility Option: Postpartum Extension, LARC Uptake, and Interpregnancy Intervals](postpartum-extension-chronic-care.pdf)

The post-Dobbs wave of state Medicaid postpartum coverage extensions (60 days → 12 months) gives leverage on how Medicaid coverage during the postpartum window affects long-acting reversible contraceptive uptake and interpregnancy intervals. This paper assembles the staggered policy panel and lays out the identification strategy for the formal evaluation, with the heterogeneity-by-state structure organized for the post-2024 outcome window when birth-certificate data become available.

### [Pricing the Golden Years: Age-Based Adoption Fee Waivers and Shelter Animal Outcomes](senior-dog-adoption-fee-waivers.pdf)

Municipal animal shelters in the U.S. increasingly waive adoption fees for older companion animals in the hope of accelerating adoption and reducing euthanasia among the hardest-to-place population. I exploit a sharp discontinuity in Austin Animal Center's posted fee schedule — under which adoption fees for dogs and cats drop from $80–$120 to zero at exactly seven years of age — to estimate the causal effect of fee elimination on adoption, live release, euthanasia, and length of stay. Using 173,775 intake records merged to outcome records over 2013–2025, I implement a sharp regression-discontinuity design with the Calonico–Cattaneo–Titiunik robust bias-corrected local-linear estimator, a triangular kernel, CCT-MSE bandwidth selection, and a donut-hole specification that accounts for integer-year heaping in age records. For dogs — the paper's headline margin — eliminating the fee has no detectable effect on any outcome (robust adoption effect +0.7 percentage points; 95% CI bounded around zero). The methodological exercise demonstrates how a sharp posted-fee schedule can be used as a regression-discontinuity running variable in a non-medical policy setting.
