## Dissertation Papers

The three papers that comprise my dissertation, *Administrative Architecture and Medicaid: Essays on Barriers, Burdens, and Bargaining*.

### 1. [Fear as a Barrier to Coverage — Public Charge Chilling Effects](public-charge-chilling.pdf)
*Dissertation Paper 1 — Job Market Paper.*
When the Trump administration expanded the "public charge" rule in 2019 — making Medicaid enrollment count against green-card applicants — many lawfully eligible immigrant families dropped coverage out of fear. Using restricted-access California health interview data, this paper measures the size of the chilling effect, identifies which subgroups were affected most, and tests whether immigration-enforcement intensity in a family's county made the chill worse. The chill is real, concentrated in mixed-status families, and tracks county-level enforcement.

### 2. [Suspension vs. Termination — Medicaid Continuity and Reentry Outcomes](suspension-termination.pdf)
*Dissertation Paper 2.*
When someone enters prison, most states terminate their Medicaid; a growing minority suspend it instead, so coverage automatically reactivates at release. This paper compares health and re-incarceration outcomes for people leaving prison in states that suspend versus terminate, asking whether this administrative choice meaningfully affects post-release access to care.

### 3. [Pooling Is Not Enough — Medicaid Drug Purchasing Pools, Pharmacy-Benefit Governance, and Supplemental Rebate Capture](medicaid-drug-pools.pdf)
*Dissertation Paper 3. Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
Most US states have joined an interstate Medicaid drug purchasing pool — SSDC, NMPI, or TOP — to negotiate larger drug rebates. This paper asks whether pool membership actually raises supplemental rebate capture, or whether the apparent gains are driven by other simultaneous reforms states make (centralizing preferred drug lists, carving pharmacy out of managed care). The answer: pool membership alone does essentially nothing; gains concentrate in states that adopt the full bundled governance package.

## Manuscripts in Review or Prepared for Submission

Single-author papers prepared for submission to *Health Affairs* and *Health Affairs Scholar*, ordered by my assessment of academic strength.

### 1. [Removing the Gate — Medicaid Prior Authorization for Buprenorphine and Prescribing](buprenorphine-pa.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
When state Medicaid programs eliminated prior-authorization requirements for buprenorphine (the leading medication for opioid use disorder), did prescribing actually increase? This paper exploits the staggered rollout of state PA-removal policies to measure whether the bureaucratic barrier was really constraining access, with magnitudes relevant to the federal removal of x-waiver and PA requirements.

### 2. [Electronic Visit Verification and Medicaid Home Care](evv-rollout.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
The 21st Century Cures Act required state Medicaid programs to install Electronic Visit Verification systems for home-based personal care services. This paper measures whether the federally-mandated rollout changed home-care spending, beneficiary utilization, and provider participation, with attention to whether monitoring technology hurt the workforce that delivers home care.

### 3. [Coverage Changes After California's 2024 Medi-Cal Adult Expansion](ca-medi-cal-2024-expansion.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs Scholar.*
California became the first US state to extend full-scope Medicaid to all income-eligible undocumented adults aged 26–49 starting January 1, 2024. This paper uses synthetic-control methods to measure whether the expansion delivered measurable coverage gains for California relative to comparable states. (Also available as [ca-medi-cal-26-49-expansion.pdf](ca-medi-cal-26-49-expansion.pdf) — same paper, alternate filename.)

## Working Papers

In rough order of my assessment of academic strength — publishability at top venues, novelty of design, robustness of identification, and substantive importance — strongest first. Most have already cleared internal pseudo-referee review; the bottom of the list contains earlier-phase or descriptive work.

### 1. [When the Office Closes — SSA Field Office Closures, the SSI-Medicaid Linkage, and Medicaid Coverage Among Disabled Adults](ssa-office-closures-medicaid.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to AEJ: Applied Economics.*
When the Social Security Administration closes a local field office, it becomes harder for low-income disabled adults to navigate the SSI application process — which is the gateway to Medicaid coverage in most states. This paper uses the staggered closing of SSA field offices as a quasi-natural experiment to measure how administrative friction translates into lost Medicaid coverage, with implications for the design of federal-state safety-net linkages.

### 2. [Section 1108's Long Shadow — Sixty Years of Capped Medicaid in the U.S. Territories, 1968–2024](territorial-medicaid-caps.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to The Milbank Quarterly, JHPPL, and Health Affairs Scholar.*
Unlike US states, the five territories (Puerto Rico, USVI, Guam, American Samoa, CNMI) operate Medicaid under a fixed dollar cap rather than open-ended federal matching. This paper traces sixty years of that cap regime through three identifying shifts (1968 imposition, 2011 ACA partial uncapping, 2019 cliff), then asks the reverse question: if a 1968-style cap were applied to mainland states today, how many infant deaths would result? Under published Medicaid-mortality elasticities, the implied range is 4,500–14,800 excess infant deaths per year nationally — a magnitude directly relevant to ongoing per-capita-cap legislative debates.

### 3. [A House Divided — Modern Heterogeneous-Effects Estimators and the Contested Mortality Effects of Hospital Desegregation, 1959–1988](title-vi-desegregation-adjudication.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Review of Economics and Statistics.*
Two recent papers using the same hospital-desegregation data reached opposite conclusions about whether Title VI's hospital-integration enforcement reduced Black infant mortality. This paper applies the newest generation of difference-in-differences estimators to adjudicate the disagreement, identifying which design choices drive the divergence and recovering a reconciled answer that respects what the data can and cannot say.

### 4. [Health Insurance for the Undocumented — State Medicaid for Non-Citizens](noncitizen-coverage.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
A handful of states have used their own funds to extend Medicaid-equivalent coverage to undocumented adults. This paper measures whether those state expansions translated into measurable coverage gains for the target population, using a state-level synthetic-control design and the limited national survey data that captures noncitizen coverage status.

### 5. [State Medicaid Doula Coverage and Birth Outcomes — Staggered Difference-in-Differences, 2019–2024](doula-coverage.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
A growing number of state Medicaid programs now cover doula services — labor-support workers who improve birth experiences for Black and low-income mothers. This paper uses the staggered rollout of state Medicaid doula coverage to measure its effect on race-stratified maternal and infant outcomes, with attention to whether the policy reaches the populations it was designed to serve.

### 6. [Medicaid Expansion, First-Year Coverage Gains, and a Bounded Mortality Null After Prison Release](post-release-mortality.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
Does Medicaid expansion reduce mortality for people released from prison — a population with extraordinarily high post-release death rates? This paper measures both the first-year coverage gain and the mortality response, and reports an honest null: the coverage gain is real and measurable, but any mortality effect is not statistically distinguishable from zero in publicly available data. The null is bounded to be small rather than confirming a large effect.

### 7. [The 1972 §1902(f) Wedge — A Fifty-Year Border-Pair Look at Medicaid Coverage, SSI Uptake, and Mortality in Eleven "209(b)" States](ssi-1972-209b-border-rd.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
The 1972 federalization of SSI created a hidden split: most states tied Medicaid eligibility to SSI receipt, but eleven so-called "209(b)" states kept their own, more restrictive criteria. This paper exploits adjacent county pairs straddling 209(b) and non-209(b) state borders to measure how the half-century-old policy choice has affected Medicaid coverage, SSI uptake, and mortality among elderly and disabled adults.

### 8. [Federally Qualified Health Centers and County Uninsurance — Shift-Share Inference with an Honest First-Stage Null](fqhc-nap-bartik-iv.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research (Methods Article).*
The federal New Access Point grant program funds new community health centers in low-income areas. This paper attempts a shift-share instrumental variables identification of the FQHC effect on county uninsurance, and then transparently reports that the instrument has a weak first stage in this setting — converting the null finding into a methods contribution about when shift-share inference is and is not appropriate for federal grant programs.

### 9. [Who Responds When Children's Medicaid Becomes Continuous?](continuous-eligibility-children.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to AEJ: Applied Economics.*
A handful of states have obtained §1115 waivers extending children's Medicaid eligibility to multi-year continuous coverage. The pooled effect on parental labor supply is null, but the parents-with-≤-high-school-education subgroup responds meaningfully — consistent with a credit-constrained / non-cash-time-substitution interpretation that other subgroups don't fit.

### 10. [The Medicaid IMD Exclusion and the Decline of State Mental Hospitals](imd-exclusion-deinstitutionalization.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
The 1965 "IMD exclusion" barred Medicaid payment for psychiatric hospital stays for adults aged 22–64 — a rule frequently blamed for the rapid post-1965 decline of state mental hospitals. This paper uses a continuous-intensity staggered difference-in-differences design and newly digitized NIMH census data to test whether the exclusion actually drove the decline, separating policy from broader deinstitutionalization currents.

### 11. [State Medicaid Coverage Decisions and GLP-1 Utilization for Obesity](glp1-coverage.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
Seventeen states currently cover GLP-1 drugs (semaglutide, tirzepatide) for obesity through Medicaid; the rest do not. This paper uses a staggered DiD and triple-difference design to measure how state coverage decisions translate into utilization differences for an extraordinarily expensive new drug class, with implications for the rapidly-evolving federal coverage debate.

### 12. [When Work Requirements Met the States — Two Case Studies of Section 1115 Medicaid Community Engagement Mandates in Arkansas and Georgia](work-requirements.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
Most state Medicaid work-requirement waivers were enjoined before implementation; Arkansas (2018) and Georgia (2023) are the only two that have actually bound. This paper documents what happened in each case — coverage losses, churn, racial heterogeneity in disenrollment — as a structured precedent for the federal work-requirement proposals re-emerging in 2025–2026.

### 13. [State Nursing-Home Staffing Floors and the Distributional Bite](nh-staffing-floor-bite.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research.*
A few states have imposed minimum nurse-staffing-hour requirements on nursing homes, with the federal government considering similar mandates. This paper uses a continuous-intensity design to measure not just average effects, but where the policy bites hardest: nursing homes serving more Medicaid patients and rural facilities absorb the costs differently than higher-margin urban facilities.

### 14. [Federal Renewal-Compliance Enforcement and Medicaid Coverage Retention](unwind-compliance.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research.*
During the 2023 Medicaid unwinding, CMS issued targeted enforcement notices to states whose procedural-disenrollment patterns suggested non-compliance with federal renewal rules. This paper uses the timing of those notices as a quasi-experiment to measure whether enforcement actually changed state behavior and improved coverage retention for the affected populations.

### 15. [State-Directed Payments in Medicaid Managed Care — A Pre-OBBBA Pooled Null on Hospital Financial Effects](state-directed-payments-panel.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research.*
State-Directed Payments (SDPs) have grown rapidly as a state Medicaid financing tool — and were targeted for restriction in the 2026 federal budget legislation. This paper assembles the public SDP preprints panel and reports an honest pooled null on hospital financial effects, with heterogeneity by provider type that suggests SDPs are not the broad hospital-rescue tool they are sometimes described as.

### 16. [Bans Without Bite — State Spread-Pricing Laws and Medicaid Generic Drug Spending](pbm-spread-pricing.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research.*
A wave of states have passed laws banning Pharmacy Benefit Managers (PBMs) from "spread pricing" inside Medicaid managed care. This paper uses staggered DiD on public Medicaid pharmacy expenditure data to test whether the bans actually moved generic drug spending — and reports a transparent null, suggesting the bans have not delivered the savings their proponents promised.

### 17. [Setup Leaves Public Traces — A Public-Data Linkage Playbook for Medicaid Program Integrity](public-data-medicaid-fraud-playbook.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs Scholar.*
Many Medicaid fraud schemes leave traces in publicly available datasets — corporate registry filings, EHR contract awards, NPI registrations, OIG exclusion lists — well before formal investigations begin. This methods paper builds a structured linkage playbook for investigative reporters, state Medicaid integrity units, and academic researchers to surface fraud-relevant patterns from public data alone.

### 18. [Heterogeneous Compliance with the FTC Express Scripts Consent Order](express-scripts-ftc-settlement.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs Scholar.*
The 2024 FTC consent order against Express Scripts required tier-equivalence between branded insulin and lower-priced biosimilar alternatives on Medicaid formularies. This paper uses pair-level insulin formulary snapshots from the first post-settlement plan year to measure who actually complied with the order and who did not, with implications for the design of pharmacy-pricing remedies.

### 19. [Trading Inpatient Beds for Uncertain Margins — Rural Emergency Hospital Conversions](rural-emergency-hospitals.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research and AJPH (Rural Special Section).*
The 2023 Rural Emergency Hospital (REH) designation lets struggling rural hospitals close their inpatient services and survive on enhanced federal payments. This paper measures what happens at hospitals that convert: inpatient capacity falls sharply (the mechanism, p < 0.001), while hospital finances are not yet decisively improved — a publishable mechanism finding with an honest inconclusive financial verdict.

### 20. [Paperwork, Proof, and Pediatric Coverage — The 2006 Citizenship Documentation Rule as an Administrative Friction Shock](dra-2006-citizenship-friction.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research.*
The 2006 Deficit Reduction Act required all Medicaid applicants to produce documentary proof of US citizenship — an administrative friction that disproportionately affected eligible citizens with weak documentation infrastructure. This paper uses a difference-in-discontinuities design at the rule's effective date to measure how the friction shock translated into pediatric coverage losses.

### 21. [Putting Teeth Into Medicaid — State Adult Dental Benefit Expansions and Population-Level Utilization](adult-dental.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research.*
A wave of states have added adult dental benefits to Medicaid. This paper uses BRFSS dental-utilization data and a staggered short-panel DiD design to test whether the expansions actually moved population-level dental visits — and reports a transparent honest null, with measurement limitations and BRFSS-2024 power considerations honestly disclosed.

### 22. [Coverage Effects of Oregon's All-Ages Medicaid Expansion](oregon-healthier-oregon-sdid.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs Scholar.*
Oregon's Healthier Oregon program extended Medicaid to all income-eligible adults regardless of immigration status. This paper uses synthetic-control methods to measure Oregon's coverage gains relative to comparable states, with explicit disclosure of the OHP Bridge program's role in the bookkeeping.

### 23. [Re-examining the OBRA Medicaid Pregnancy Expansions](obra-pregnancy-expansion-update.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research (Research Brief).*
The OBRA 1986/87/89/90 Medicaid pregnancy expansions are a foundational result in the Medicaid-and-mortality literature. This paper replicates the canonical Currie-Gruber simulated-bite design using modern treatment-intensity event-study methods on linked NCHS natality and birth-infant-death data — and reports an honest null where the original literature found large effects, with implications for how we read the rest of the foundational Medicaid-and-mortality evidence base.

### 24. [The Procedural Wedge — Medicaid Disenrollment and Community Health Center Finances During the Unwinding](administrative-wedge.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research (Research Brief).*
Did the procedural-disenrollment-heavy 2023 Medicaid unwinding hurt the financial health of FQHCs that disproportionately serve newly-disenrolled patients? This paper builds a cross-sectional design with a structured null-first-stage methods note: the instrument is reframed as a descriptive supply-side exhibit when its first stage proves too weak to support causal inference, and the contribution becomes methodological.

### 25. [FMAP Floor and Medicaid LTSS Rebalancing — A Methods Note](fmap-floor-ltss-rkd.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Medical Care.*
The federal Medicaid Assistance Percentage (FMAP) has a statutory 50% floor that creates an attractive regression-kink-design discontinuity for studying Medicaid Long-Term Services and Supports rebalancing. This paper documents why the kink-design estimator is highly sensitive to fixed-effect specification in this setting and what authors should disclose when using FMAP-kink RKD on public data.

### 26. [Did Losing Medicaid Make Anyone Sicker? A Composition / Null Reading of the Post-Pandemic Unwinding](unwinding-adult-health.pdf)
*Under revision.*
A composition / null reading of whether adult coverage loss during the 2023 unwinding produced detectable adult-health decline in publicly available state-level surveys. The paper's headline is the absence of a detectable health-decline signal once survey composition shifts are accounted for, with implications for how aggressive procedural disenrollment is interpreted by policy audiences.
