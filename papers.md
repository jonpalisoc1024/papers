## Dissertation Papers

The three papers that comprise my dissertation, *Administrative Architecture and Medicaid: Essays on Barriers, Burdens, and Bargaining*.

### [Fear as a Barrier to Coverage — Public Charge Chilling Effects](public-charge-chilling.pdf)
*Dissertation Paper 1 — Job Market Paper.*
When the Trump administration expanded the "public charge" rule in 2019 — making Medicaid enrollment count against green-card applicants — many lawfully eligible immigrant families dropped coverage out of fear. Using restricted-access California health interview data and a difference-in-differences design around the August 2019 rule, this paper measures the chilling effect, identifies which subgroups bore it, and tests whether immigration-enforcement intensity in a family's county made the chill worse. The chill is large and persistent for mixed-status families with U.S.-citizen children, attenuated for fully documented households, and tracks county-level enforcement intensity — evidence that the rule's effect operated through fear rather than through legal eligibility changes.

### [Suspension vs. Termination — Medicaid Continuity and Reentry Outcomes](suspension-termination.pdf)
*Dissertation Paper 2.*
When someone enters prison, most states terminate their Medicaid; a growing minority suspend it instead, so coverage automatically reactivates at release. Using a staggered policy panel of state suspension adoptions and a difference-in-differences design on linked corrections-and-Medicaid data, this paper compares post-release Medicaid uptake, care utilization, and adverse outcomes between people leaving prison in suspension versus termination states. Suspension states recover post-release coverage substantially faster and produce measurable utilization differences in the immediate post-release window, with the strongest effects in the first thirty days — the period when reentry mortality risk peaks.

### [Pooling Is Not Enough — Medicaid Drug Purchasing Pools, Pharmacy-Benefit Governance, and Supplemental Rebate Capture](medicaid-drug-pools.pdf)
*Dissertation Paper 3. Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
Most US states have joined an interstate Medicaid drug purchasing pool — SSDC, NMPI, or TOP — to negotiate larger drug rebates. Combining a 2002–2024 CMS-64 rebate panel with a new spell-coded 50-state pharmacy-benefit governance dataset and a dynamic event-study design, this paper asks whether pool membership actually raises supplemental rebate capture, or whether the apparent gains are really driven by other simultaneous reforms states make. Pool membership alone does essentially nothing (any-pool standalone effect 0.06 pp, p = 0.95; the SSDC benchmark of 3.37 pp falls to 1.28 pp after governance controls). Gains concentrate in states that simultaneously pool, run a uniform preferred drug list, and carve pharmacy out of managed care — a 2.22-percentage-point dynamic increase from a 1.75-percentage-point pre-bundle baseline, or roughly a 127 percent relative gain.

## Manuscripts in Review or Prepared for Submission

Single-author papers prepared for submission to *Health Affairs* and *Health Affairs Scholar*, ordered by my assessment of academic strength.

### [Removing the Gate — Medicaid Prior Authorization for Buprenorphine and Prescribing](buprenorphine-pa.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
When state Medicaid programs eliminated prior-authorization requirements for buprenorphine — the leading medication for opioid use disorder — did prescribing actually increase? Using the staggered rollout of state PA-removal policies and a difference-in-differences design on State Drug Utilization Data, this paper measures whether the bureaucratic barrier was constraining access at the state-Medicaid margin. Results indicate modest but precisely-estimated increases in Medicaid buprenorphine prescribing in PA-removal states, concentrated in primary-care and rural prescribers; the policy-relevant magnitude is informative for the federal removal of x-waiver and ongoing PA requirements but is smaller than advocacy estimates would predict.

### [Electronic Visit Verification and Medicaid Home Care](evv-rollout.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
The 21st Century Cures Act required state Medicaid programs to install Electronic Visit Verification (EVV) systems for home-based personal-care services. Using the staggered state EVV-implementation timeline and Medicaid administrative-claims data, this paper measures whether the federally-mandated rollout changed home-care spending, beneficiary utilization, and provider participation. The headline result is a bounded null on personal-care utilization with secondary evidence that smaller home-care providers exited the market disproportionately, suggesting EVV's compliance burden shifted who delivers home care more than it changed whether the care happens.

### [Coverage Changes After California's 2024 Medi-Cal Adult Expansion](ca-medi-cal-2024-expansion.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs Scholar.*
California became the first US state to extend full-scope Medicaid to all income-eligible undocumented adults aged 26–49 starting January 1, 2024. Using synthetic-control methods on ACS coverage data and a 1–200% FPL working-age sample, this paper measures Medicaid coverage gains in California relative to a donor pool of comparable states. The first-year coverage gain is positive and policy-coherent in size, but the placebo-rank diagnostic is imprecise enough that I read it as a directional result rather than a definitive estimate. (Also available as [ca-medi-cal-26-49-expansion.pdf](ca-medi-cal-26-49-expansion.pdf) — same paper, alternate filename.)

## Working Papers

In rough order of my assessment of academic strength — publishability at top venues, novelty of design, robustness of identification, and substantive importance — strongest first. Most have already cleared internal pseudo-referee review; the bottom of the list contains earlier-phase or descriptive work.

### [When the Office Closes — SSA Field Office Closures, the SSI–Medicaid Linkage, and Medicaid Coverage Among Disabled Adults](ssa-office-closures-medicaid.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to AEJ: Applied Economics.*
When the Social Security Administration closes a local field office, it becomes harder for low-income disabled adults to navigate the SSI application process — and SSI receipt is the gateway to Medicaid coverage in most states. Using the staggered closing of SSA field offices and a difference-in-differences design that exploits commuting-zone-level exposure to closures, this paper measures how administrative friction translates into lost Medicaid coverage among the disabled population. Office closures produce measurable, durable declines in SSI receipt and adult Medicaid coverage in the affected commuting zones, with the bulk of the effect on first-time applicants rather than on existing beneficiaries — evidence that the field-office network functions as a coverage on-ramp rather than as an ongoing support.

### [Section 1108's Long Shadow — Sixty Years of Capped Medicaid in the U.S. Territories, 1968–2024](territorial-medicaid-caps.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to The Milbank Quarterly, JHPPL, and Health Affairs Scholar.*
Unlike US states, the five territories (Puerto Rico, USVI, Guam, American Samoa, CNMI) operate Medicaid under a fixed dollar cap rather than open-ended federal matching. This paper traces sixty years of that cap regime through three identifying shifts (the 1968 imposition of Section 1108(c), the 2011 ACA partial uncapping, the 2019 cliff) and then asks the reverse question: if a 1968-scaled cap were applied to mainland states today, what would the mortality magnitude be? The historical Puerto Rico arms produce informative nulls — read honestly as level-mismatch nulls, because Puerto Rico's 1959–1967 mean infant mortality of 42.7 per 1,000 sat 13.3 points above the donor-pool mean and 3.4 points above even Mississippi, making mechanical demographic-transition convergence dominate the post-1968 trajectory. The reverse counterfactual, in contrast, is concrete: a 1968-scaled cap on the fifty states would remove $513 billion in federal Medicaid in FY2024 and imply 4,500–14,800 excess infant deaths per year under two peer-reviewed mortality elasticities; Graham–Cassidy 2017 would imply 530–1,800 excess infant deaths annually.

### [A House Divided — Modern Heterogeneous-Effects Estimators and the Contested Mortality Effects of Hospital Desegregation, 1959–1988](title-vi-desegregation-adjudication.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Review of Economics and Statistics.*
Two recent papers using the same hospital-desegregation data reached opposite conclusions about whether Title VI's hospital-integration enforcement reduced Black infant mortality. Applying the full menu of modern heterogeneity-robust difference-in-differences estimators — Callaway–Sant'Anna, Sun–Abraham, Borusyak–Jaravel–Spiess, de Chaisemartin–D'Haultfœuille — alongside synthetic-control and HonestDiD sensitivity bounds, this paper adjudicates the disagreement by isolating which design choices drive the divergent estimates. The reconciled answer recovers a modest, positive, but smaller-than-the-prior-large-claim mortality effect of Title VI enforcement on Black infants, and identifies the specific contamination patterns under TWFE that produced one side of the original disagreement.

### [Health Insurance for the Undocumented — State Medicaid for Non-Citizens](noncitizen-coverage.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
A handful of states have used their own funds to extend Medicaid-equivalent coverage to undocumented adults. Using a state-level synthetic-control design on ACS and CHIS coverage data restricted to noncitizen subsamples, this paper measures whether those state expansions translated into measurable coverage gains for the target population. The expansion states show clear coverage gains relative to synthetic controls, with the largest gains in the 26–49 age band that most state expansions target; the analysis honestly notes that survey undercoverage of undocumented adults bounds what the public data can establish about the population most affected.

### [State Medicaid Doula Coverage and Birth Outcomes — Staggered Difference-in-Differences, 2019–2024](doula-coverage.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
A growing number of state Medicaid programs cover doula services — labor-support workers who improve birth experiences for Black and low-income mothers. Using the staggered rollout of state Medicaid doula coverage and a race-stratified Callaway–Sant'Anna event study on birth-certificate data, this paper measures effects on cesarean-section rates, low-birthweight rates, and prenatal-care utilization. The headline finding is a meaningful reduction in cesarean rates concentrated among Black mothers in covering states, with smaller effects on other outcomes — evidence consistent with the targeting story doula advocates have made.

### [Medicaid Expansion, First-Year Coverage Gains, and a Bounded Mortality Null After Prison Release](post-release-mortality.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
Does Medicaid expansion reduce mortality for people released from prison — a population with extraordinarily high post-release death rates? Linking state correction-release records to mortality follow-up via a difference-in-differences design around the ACA Medicaid expansions, this paper measures both the first-year coverage gain and the mortality response. The first-year coverage gain is large and precisely estimated; the mortality response is an honest null whose confidence interval bounds the effect to be small. The combined finding is informative — the coverage on-ramp works but does not, by itself, deliver the mortality reduction that the underlying public-health intuition would suggest in a one-year window.

### [The 1972 §1902(f) Wedge — A Fifty-Year Border-Pair Look at Medicaid Coverage, SSI Uptake, and Mortality in Eleven "209(b)" States](ssi-1972-209b-border-rd.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
The 1972 federalization of SSI created a hidden split: most states tied Medicaid eligibility to SSI receipt, but eleven so-called "209(b)" states kept their own, more restrictive criteria. Using adjacent county pairs straddling 209(b) and non-209(b) state borders and fifty years of administrative data, this paper measures how the half-century-old policy choice has shaped Medicaid coverage, SSI uptake, and mortality among elderly and disabled adults. 209(b) states show persistently lower SSI uptake and meaningful coverage gaps at the border, with the mortality gradient informative but underpowered to support a definitive causal claim — a "policy chose decades ago to leave coverage on the table" finding.

### [Federally Qualified Health Centers and County Uninsurance — Shift-Share Inference with an Honest First-Stage Null](fqhc-nap-bartik-iv.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research (Methods Article).*
The federal New Access Point grant program funds new community health centers in low-income areas. This paper attempts a shift-share instrumental-variables identification of the FQHC effect on county uninsurance, building a Bartik-style instrument from baseline FQHC composition interacted with national grant-flow trends. The first stage is honestly too weak in this setting to support causal inference, and the paper converts that finding into a methods contribution about when shift-share inference is and is not appropriate for federal grant programs — with explicit guidance on which exclusion-restriction failures are diagnosable in public data.

### [Who Responds When Children's Medicaid Becomes Continuous?](continuous-eligibility-children.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to AEJ: Applied Economics.*
A handful of states have obtained §1115 waivers extending children's Medicaid eligibility to multi-year continuous coverage. Using the staggered policy panel and a heterogeneity-robust event study on CPS labor-supply data with month-correct treatment timing, this paper asks whether parents adjust their work behavior when their children's Medicaid no longer requires annual paperwork. The pooled labor-supply effect is null. But the parents-with-high-school-education-or-less subgroup responds meaningfully, with hours and labor-force participation both moving in the direction a credit-constrained / non-cash-time-substitution story would predict — heterogeneity that the pooled estimate hides.

### [The Medicaid IMD Exclusion and the Decline of State Mental Hospitals](imd-exclusion-deinstitutionalization.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
The 1965 "IMD exclusion" barred Medicaid payment for psychiatric hospital stays for adults aged 22–64 — a rule frequently blamed for the rapid post-1965 decline of state mental hospitals. Using a continuous-intensity staggered difference-in-differences design on newly digitized NIMH biennial inpatient census data, this paper tests whether the exclusion actually drove the decline or whether it merely coincided with broader deinstitutionalization currents. States with higher exposure to the exclusion (measured by pre-rule Medicaid-IMD penetration) did experience faster post-1965 declines in state-mental-hospital census, with the effect concentrated in the first decade after the rule and attenuating thereafter — consistent with the exclusion accelerating, but not solely causing, the deinstitutionalization wave.

### [State Medicaid Coverage Decisions and GLP-1 Utilization for Obesity](glp1-coverage.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
Seventeen states currently cover GLP-1 drugs (semaglutide, tirzepatide) for obesity through Medicaid; the rest do not. Using a staggered difference-in-differences and triple-difference design on State Drug Utilization Data 2022–2025, this paper measures how state coverage decisions translate into prescribing differences for an extraordinarily expensive new drug class. Covering states see large, precisely-estimated increases in Medicaid GLP-1 utilization with no detectable substitution away from older diabetes therapies — evidence that the coverage decision is the binding margin and that the resulting state Medicaid fiscal load grows in lockstep with utilization.

### [When Work Requirements Met the States — Two Case Studies of Section 1115 Medicaid Community Engagement Mandates in Arkansas and Georgia](work-requirements.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs.*
Most state Medicaid work-requirement waivers were enjoined before implementation; Arkansas (2018) and Georgia (2023) are the only two that have actually bound. This paper documents each case carefully — coverage losses, churn patterns, racial heterogeneity in disenrollment, the role of the reporting infrastructure each state built — as a structured precedent for the federal work-requirement proposals re-emerging in 2025–2026. Arkansas's 18,000-person coverage loss was concentrated in Black enrollees who were nominally already meeting the work requirement but failed the reporting infrastructure; Georgia's much smaller Pathways enrollment failed to come close to the projected scale-up.

### [State Nursing-Home Staffing Floors and the Distributional Bite](nh-staffing-floor-bite.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research.*
A few states have imposed minimum nurse-staffing-hour requirements on nursing homes, with the federal government considering similar mandates. This paper uses a continuous-intensity design on facility-level CMS data across eleven analytic states to measure not just average effects but where the policy bites hardest — nursing homes serving more Medicaid patients and rural facilities absorb the costs differently than higher-margin urban facilities. The heterogeneity finding is the contribution: average effects mask large distributional asymmetries that policy designers should know about before scaling the federal rule.

### [Federal Renewal-Compliance Enforcement and Medicaid Coverage Retention](unwind-compliance.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research.*
During the 2023 Medicaid unwinding, CMS issued targeted enforcement notices to states whose procedural-disenrollment patterns suggested non-compliance with federal renewal rules. Using the timing of those notices as a quasi-experiment on monthly state Medicaid enrollment data, this paper measures whether enforcement actually changed state behavior and improved coverage retention. Enforced states show meaningful improvements in renewal completion and reductions in procedural disenrollment in the months after a CMS notice, with the largest effects in states that had been the most aggressive procedurally — evidence that federal oversight, applied to a measurable and discrete trigger, can move state Medicaid administration in real time.

### [State-Directed Payments in Medicaid Managed Care — A Pre-OBBBA Pooled Null on Hospital Financial Effects](state-directed-payments-panel.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research.*
State-Directed Payments (SDPs) have grown rapidly as a state Medicaid financing tool — and were targeted for restriction in the 2026 federal budget legislation. Assembling the public SDP preprints panel and matching to hospital Medicare cost-report financial data, this paper reports an honest pooled null on hospital financial effects, with heterogeneity by provider type that suggests SDPs are not the broad hospital-rescue tool they are sometimes described as. Academic medical centers absorb most of the targeted SDP flow, while smaller community hospitals see little measurable financial benefit — informative for the OBBBA-era debate over how aggressively to restrict SDPs.

### [Bans Without Bite — State Spread-Pricing Laws and Medicaid Generic Drug Spending](pbm-spread-pricing.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research.*
A wave of states have passed laws banning Pharmacy Benefit Managers (PBMs) from "spread pricing" inside Medicaid managed care. Using staggered DiD on public Medicaid pharmacy expenditure data and a Goodman-Bacon decomposition with bootstrap-corrected standard errors, this paper tests whether the bans actually moved generic drug spending. The pooled effect is a transparent null with confidence intervals that exclude the savings claims made in the legislative-history record — suggesting that PBM spread is either smaller than advocates argue or that the PBM market routes around the bans through other channels.

### [Setup Leaves Public Traces — A Public-Data Linkage Playbook for Medicaid Program Integrity](public-data-medicaid-fraud-playbook.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs Scholar.*
Many Medicaid fraud schemes leave traces in publicly available datasets — corporate registry filings, EHR contract awards, NPI registrations, OIG exclusion lists — well before formal investigations begin. This methods paper builds a structured linkage playbook for investigative reporters, state Medicaid program-integrity units, and academic researchers, demonstrating the workflow on three recent high-profile Medicaid fraud cases where the public-data trail predated the formal indictment by 12–36 months. The contribution is a reproducible playbook rather than a single causal estimate.

### [Heterogeneous Compliance with the FTC Express Scripts Consent Order](express-scripts-ftc-settlement.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs Scholar.*
The 2024 FTC consent order against Express Scripts required tier-equivalence between branded insulin and lower-priced biosimilar alternatives on Medicaid formularies. Using pair-level insulin formulary snapshots from the first post-settlement plan year and a pair-panel design, this paper measures who actually complied with the order and who did not. The headline tier-difference coefficient is large and statistically significant (β = −83 percentage points, SE 4, n = 502 pairs), but permutation inference confirms substantial heterogeneity in compliance across plans — evidence that the order moved the average but that targeted enforcement on the non-compliant subset remains the binding constraint on patient access.

### [Trading Inpatient Beds for Uncertain Margins — Rural Emergency Hospital Conversions](rural-emergency-hospitals.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research and AJPH (Rural Special Section).*
The 2023 Rural Emergency Hospital (REH) designation lets struggling rural hospitals close their inpatient services and survive on enhanced federal payments. Using a Callaway–Sant'Anna event study on CMS-trimmed REH conversions in the first program year, this paper measures what happens at converting hospitals: inpatient capacity falls sharply and significantly (the mechanism, p < 0.001), while year-one hospital operating margins move in a positive direction but are not decisively improved (primary CS-trimmed +7.4 pp, p = 0.21). The publishable headline is the mechanism finding, with the financial verdict reported as honest year-one inconclusiveness — a useful baseline for the longer post-window evaluations the federal program will eventually permit.

### [Paperwork, Proof, and Pediatric Coverage — The 2006 Citizenship Documentation Rule as an Administrative Friction Shock](dra-2006-citizenship-friction.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research.*
The 2006 Deficit Reduction Act required all Medicaid applicants to produce documentary proof of U.S. citizenship — an administrative friction that disproportionately affected eligible citizens with weak documentation infrastructure. Using a difference-in-discontinuities design at the rule's effective date and a citizen-aware interrupted-time-series, this paper measures how the friction shock translated into pediatric Medicaid coverage losses. The post-rule coverage drop is concentrated in low-income citizen children whose families needed time to produce passports or birth certificates, with a partial recovery as documentation infrastructure improved — consistent with friction-as-eligibility-tax rather than as a screen for ineligible enrollees.

### [Putting Teeth Into Medicaid — State Adult Dental Benefit Expansions and Population-Level Utilization](adult-dental.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research.*
A wave of states have added adult dental benefits to Medicaid. Using BRFSS 2016–2023 dental-utilization data and a staggered short-panel DiD design, this paper tests whether the expansions actually moved population-level dental visits. The pooled effect is an honest null with bounded confidence intervals; the paper transparently flags BRFSS-2024's later release as the cleanest forward power check. The contribution is to set a defensible "what we currently can and cannot detect" baseline that future BRFSS releases will sharpen, rather than to overclaim from BRFSS-2023 alone.

### [Coverage Effects of Oregon's All-Ages Medicaid Expansion](oregon-healthier-oregon-sdid.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Affairs Scholar.*
Oregon's Healthier Oregon program extended Medicaid to all income-eligible adults regardless of immigration status. Using synthetic-control and synthetic-DiD methods on ACS coverage data restricted to the 26–54 age band at 1–200% FPL, this paper measures Oregon's coverage gains relative to comparable donor states, with explicit disclosure of how the OHP Bridge program enters the bookkeeping. The headline coverage gain in the first post-expansion year is positive and consistent with the policy's enrolled-population estimates from state administrative data, and the synthetic-DiD point estimate is robust to placebo-rotation across the donor pool.

### [Re-examining the OBRA Medicaid Pregnancy Expansions](obra-pregnancy-expansion-update.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research (Research Brief).*
The OBRA 1986/87/89/90 Medicaid pregnancy expansions are a foundational result in the Medicaid-and-mortality literature. Using the canonical Currie–Gruber simulated-bite TWFE design on linked NCHS natality and birth-infant-death data, this paper replicates the original specification with modern treatment-intensity event-study methods. The honest-null finding — where the original literature found large infant-mortality effects, the modern replication produces estimates statistically indistinguishable from zero — has implications for how we read the rest of the foundational Medicaid-and-mortality evidence base, and is framed as a methodological lesson about how staggered treatment timing and TWFE contamination can amplify older estimates.

### [The Procedural Wedge — Medicaid Disenrollment and Community Health Center Finances During the Unwinding](administrative-wedge.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Health Services Research (Research Brief).*
Did the procedural-disenrollment-heavy 2023 Medicaid unwinding hurt the financial health of FQHCs that disproportionately serve newly-disenrolled patients? This paper builds a cross-sectional design with a structured null-first-stage methods note: a state-level MACPAC instrument is reframed as a descriptive supply-side exhibit once the first stage proves too weak to support causal inference, and the contribution becomes a methods-note about how to honestly disclose null-first-stage findings in policy-relevant settings. The descriptive supply-side exhibit shows meaningful unwinding-period revenue stress at FQHCs in procedurally-aggressive states, even if the causal attribution does not survive the methods bar.

### [FMAP Floor and Medicaid LTSS Rebalancing — A Methods Note](fmap-floor-ltss-rkd.pdf)
*Prepared for submission to Medical Care.*
The federal Medicaid Assistance Percentage (FMAP) has a statutory 50% floor that creates an attractive regression-kink-design discontinuity for studying Medicaid Long-Term Services and Supports rebalancing. This paper documents — under exact two-way state and fiscal-year fixed-effect residualization on the FY2008–FY2023 public-data panel — why the kink-design estimator is highly sensitive to fixed-effect specification in this setting and what authors should disclose when using FMAP-kink RKD on public data. The original headline RKD result is withdrawn; the methods-note framing for fixed-effect-residualization sensitivity is the publishable contribution.

### [Did Losing Medicaid Make Anyone Sicker? A Composition / Null Reading of the Post-Pandemic Unwinding](unwinding-adult-health.pdf)
*Under revision.*
Did the 2023 Medicaid unwinding produce detectable adult-health decline in publicly available state-level surveys? Using BRFSS and ACS time-series with state-level procedural-aggression measures, this paper tests for adult-health-decline signals across self-reported health, days-of-poor-health, and uninsurance. The composition-corrected reading is an absence of a detectable health-decline signal in publicly available data — a finding that does not vindicate the unwinding so much as it disciplines what publicly available state-level surveys can and cannot establish about a policy whose adverse effects may operate through channels invisible to short-window aggregate-survey measurement.

### [Pricing the Golden Years — Age-Based Adoption Fee Waivers on Shelter Animal Outcomes](senior-dog-adoption-fee-waivers.pdf)
