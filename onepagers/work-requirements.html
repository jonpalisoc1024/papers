Policy One-Pager Medicaid Work Requirements
Working Paper · 2026

Work requirements: coverage loss without employment gain.

The Arkansas, New Hampshire, and Georgia experiences are the closest natural experiments. The verdict is consistent across estimators and outcomes.
Jonathan PalisocPh.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

Across the three implemented Medicaid work-requirement waivers, coverage among targeted adults fell by an estimated 14–17 pp, with no detectable increase in employment in any state. Compliance reporting burdens — not failure to meet hours — drove the disenrollments. The mechanism replicates the unwinding’s procedural-churn signature.

Why the mechanism matters.

Coverage loss concentrates on adults who were already working or already exempt. The binding constraint is the monthly online reporting system, not the work rule itself. This is the unwinding’s administrative-burden mechanism, applied prospectively.

A

Why it matters now.

  • Federal legislation in 2025–26 contemplates national work requirements.
  • The three implemented states are the only empirical evidence base.
  • The cost is real: ~18,000 Arkansans lost coverage with no employment offset.
B

What we did.

Synthesized CMS implementation data, state agency reports, CPS-ASEC and ACS coverage estimates, and the published peer-reviewed evaluations. Difference-in-differences and synthetic-control replications for each state.

C

Caveats.

  • Programs were short-lived; longer horizons could reveal lagged labor effects.
  • Compliance tech is improving — Georgia’s portal beats Arkansas’s.
  • A federal version would scale the same paperwork mechanism nationally.
→

So what?

The evidence is consistent: work requirements cost coverage and don’t deliver employment. Anchor the federal debate on Arkansas’s 18,000 disenrolled adults.
