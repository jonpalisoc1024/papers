Converting to survive: the Rural Emergency Hospital designation and access in rural America.

CMS’s 2023 REH designation lets rural critical-access hospitals drop inpatient care for a monthly facility payment. Conversion preserves an ED — but the inpatient withdrawal is its own access shock.
Jonathan PalisocPh.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

By end-2024, 32 hospitals had converted to REH. Conversion is concentrated in counties with rising operating losses and prior service-line reductions; converted hospitals maintain ED throughput but inpatient days fall to zero, and ambulance transfer times to the nearest inpatient facility rise by an estimated 14–26 minutes median. The designation prevents a full closure but is not access-neutral.

What the designation is.

  • Created by the Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2021; effective Jan 2023.
  • No inpatient care; 24/7 ED; max 24-hr observation.
  • Pays a monthly fixed facility payment (~$272K/month, 2024) plus a 5% Medicare outpatient add-on.
02

Who converts.

Conversion is concentrated where the inpatient line was already failing: persistent operating losses, low daily census, prior obstetric and surgical service cuts, and majority-rural counties. The selection is non-random.

The access tradeoff.

ED volume stays roughly flat at converted sites. The cost shows up downstream: transfers for inpatient-requiring conditions get longer, and ambulance origin–destination times to the nearest hospital rise materially.

A

Why it matters.

  • 136 rural hospitals closed 2010–2023; another 600+ are at risk.
  • REH was designed to be a middle path between full operation and full closure.
  • Whether the middle path delivers depends on what falls out of the inpatient withdrawal.
B

What we did.

Linked CMS POS files, Medicare cost reports, AHA Annual Survey, and state EMS run data where available. Event-study DiD around conversion date; access measures from NEMSIS-linked transfer times.

C

Caveats.

  • EMS data coverage is incomplete; transfer-time estimates are a range.
  • Counterfactual is conversion vs. closure, not vs. status quo.
  • Two-year post-period — longer-run finance and access TBD.
→

So what?

REH preserves an ED in places that would otherwise lose one entirely. But policymakers shouldn’t score it as access-neutral — the inpatient withdrawal is real, especially for OB, cardiac, and intensive care. Pair the designation with regional transfer-capacity investment.
