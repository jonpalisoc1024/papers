Healthier Oregon: a single-state coverage expansion for adults regardless of status.

Oregon’s 2022–23 phased rollout offers a clean staggered design for adult coverage among undocumented residents — one of the few state programs with a usable pre/post and within-state comparison.
Jonathan PalisocPh.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

Oregon’s Healthier Oregon program extended Medicaid-equivalent coverage to adults regardless of immigration status, phased by age cohort July 2022–July 2023. ACS-based DiD shows a coverage gain of +2.6 to +3.4 pp for low-income noncitizens, concentrated in the income- and age-eligible cell — with no detectable private crowd-out.

Within-state evidence.

The phased age boundaries give a within-state DiD without crossing state-policy borders. Coverage rises in step with each cohort’s eligibility date; trends are flat among ineligible age windows in the same period.

A

Why it matters.

  • One of the few state coverage expansions for undocumented adults with an actual staggered design.
  • Sets a defensible cost-per-covered-adult benchmark for legislatures weighing similar programs.
  • Coverage is the precondition for primary care, chronic-disease control, and prenatal access.
B

What we did.

ACS 2018–2024 microdata for adults 19–64 below 200% FPL; within-state age-cohort DiD plus a national triple-difference using non-expanding states.

Honest pre-trends (Rambachan–Roth), cluster-robust SEs by state.

C

Limitations.

  • ACS underreports undocumented status; results are a lower bound.
  • Oregon is a small denominator — pair with California (Medi-Cal age-based) for triangulation.
  • Outcomes beyond coverage (utilization, ED, mortality) await claims-linked follow-up.
→

So what?

The first cohort-staggered evidence on state-funded coverage for undocumented adults shows it does what proponents claim — modestly, and without crowding out private coverage. A clean benchmark legislatures can price.
