Working Paper · 2026

The procedural wedge: how the Medicaid unwinding squeezed community health centers.

States that hadn’t invested in automatic renewals dropped more people for paperwork reasons — and their community health centers paid the bill.
Jonathan Palisoc Ph.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

When the pandemic-era Medicaid pause ended in 2023, 25 million people lost coverage — about 69% for paperwork reasons, not because they were ineligible. States that couldn’t renew people automatically passed that cost downstream: in their community health centers, the Medicaid share of revenue fell roughly point-for-point with the procedural-disenrollment rate.

The mechanism: a $512 hole per patient.

Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs) are the nation’s largest primary-care safety net — over 32 million patients. They’re legally required to see anyone who shows up. But the math is unforgiving:

$1,418
per Medicaid patient, annually
$906
per uninsured patient, via Section 330 grants

Every patient pushed from Medicaid to uninsured strips about $512 from the center’s revenue while the visit itself still has to happen. Aggregate FQHC margins turned negative for the first time in over a decade in 2024.

A

Why it matters.

  • FQHCs serve more than 1 in 11 Americans and disproportionately care for low-income and uninsured patients.
  • Unlike hospitals, they can’t cost-shift to commercial insurers — Medicaid revenue is the model.
  • Pending federal proposals would add more Medicaid paperwork (work requirements, more frequent renewals), repeating this shock.
B

What we did.

Linked HRSA Uniform Data System records for every FQHC, 2019–2024, to state-level procedural-disenrollment rates.

Used a natural experiment: states’ pre-pandemic automatic-renewal capacity (a MACPAC tier set years earlier) strongly predicted how much paperwork churn happened during the unwinding — but had nothing to do with later FQHC finances except through that channel.

C

Why believe the result.

  • The instrument — pre-pandemic renewal IT — was built years earlier for efficiency, not in anticipation of a coverage shock.
  • Payer-mix result (Medicaid share of revenue) is the cleanest and most robust finding.
  • Caveat. Point estimates of $5.4M per center in lost revenue are upper bounds — weak-instrument-robust tests can’t fully reject zero on the dollar magnitude.
→

So what?

Paperwork is a de facto budget cut for the safety net. Eligibility rules that push more renewals onto enrollees don’t just hurt the people who fall off — they hollow out the providers left to treat them.
