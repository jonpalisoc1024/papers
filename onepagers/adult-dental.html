Putting teeth into Medicaid.

Did the 2020–23 wave of state adult dental expansions actually move utilization? The state-level data so far can’t see an effect — and that’s a finding worth taking seriously.
Jonathan Palisoc Ph.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

Across the 2020–23 wave of state Medicaid adult dental expansions, BRFSS shows a 1.18-percentage-point increase in past-year dental visits — directionally positive, but statistically indistinguishable from zero. After Romano–Wolf multiple-testing correction, no outcome in the five-outcome family clears the 5% threshold. The most useful conclusion is honest: with only nine treated state-year observations, BRFSS state-level data is underpowered for a population-diluted effect of this size.

An unprecedented wave — then a quiet result.

Adult dental has always been an optional Medicaid benefit, and historically the first thing on the chopping block in a recession. The 2020–23 window broke that pattern: enhanced federal match and a new advocacy coalition produced the largest expansion wave since 1965.

The arithmetic of a diluted signal.

The prior literature finds dental coverage raises visits by 6–13 pp — but measured among Medicaid enrollees. BRFSS measures the whole adult state population, where Medicaid covers maybe a fifth of adults. The expected state-population signal:

The minimum detectable effect at 80% power with 9 treated state-years is roughly 3.5 pp. The expected signal is ~2 pp. The study is built to miss it.

A

Why this matters now.

  • Several legislatures are weighing FY 2026 dental expansions; they want to know what the last wave bought.
  • Oral health drives downstream costs — ED visits, diabetes control, cardiovascular risk — making this a high-leverage benefit on paper.
  • A premature “it didn’t work” reading of the BRFSS null would be the wrong policy lesson.
B

The honest validity check.

To test whether BRFSS is masking a real Medicaid-only effect, I pulled the NHIS Medicaid-enrolled subsample directly.

No hidden positive effect lurking in the subpopulation — likely reflecting COVID-era disruption to dental care, not the expansions themselves.

C

What would resolve this.

  • 2024 BRFSS wave (mid-2025 release) roughly doubles the treated state-years as the 2023 cohort enters the post-period.
  • T-MSIS/TAF Medicaid claims permit direct Medicaid-only restriction — matching the unit-of-effect of the prior literature.
  • Pair state-level BRFSS with NHIS region-level estimates for triangulation.
→

So what?

Not evidence that adult dental expansions don’t work — evidence that state-aggregate survey data is the wrong instrument for a benefit touching a fifth of the population. A pre-registered null, transparently reported, beats an overpowered positive spin.
