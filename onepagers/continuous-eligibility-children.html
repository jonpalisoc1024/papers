Working Paper · 2026

Medicaid lock: when a child’s coverage stops gating a parent’s career.

Three states quietly extended Medicaid for young children to six full years. The biggest measurable effect isn’t on the kids’ coverage — it’s on their parents’ work.
Jonathan Palisoc Ph.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

When parents in Oregon, Washington, and New Mexico stopped worrying their child would lose Medicaid the moment they earned a raise, they worked +0.71 more hours per week and were +1.66 pp more likely to hold a full-time job. Annual wages rose by roughly $4,400. The effect is on the intensive margin — people already working took on more — exactly the canonical signature of Medicaid lock being released.

Labor-force participation and employment do not move. This isn’t pulling new workers in — it’s letting the parents already working take on more demanding, better-paid jobs.

02

The mechanism: a hidden marginal tax.

A parent considering a wage bump from $20 to $25 an hour doesn’t just face income tax — they risk their toddler losing Medicaid mid-year. That implicit tax holds parents in place.

The waivers sever it. Oregon (Jan 2023), Washington (Jul 2023), and New Mexico (Nov 2023) provide continuous Medicaid/CHIP eligibility from birth through age 5 — no income recheck, six full years of locked-in coverage. Pennsylvania joined in 2025.

A

Why this matters.

  • The 2023 CAA required 12-month continuous eligibility nationally as of Jan 2024. The waiver states show what multi-year CE buys.
  • Most policy discussion focuses on churn reduction for kids. The labor channel is potentially larger and almost entirely under-discussed.
  • Pennsylvania activated in 2025; several states are considering 1115 waivers. The first quasi-experimental evidence base now exists.
B

What we did.

Four data sources, four identification layers:

  • NSCH 2016–2024 · cross-state DiD on child coverage
  • ACS 2016–2024 · within-state under-6 vs 19–25 triple-difference
  • CPS-ASEC 2016–2025 · parent labor outcomes, 99.3% match rate
  • SIPP 2018–2024 · within-household sibling FE: under-6 vs. 7–12 in the same family

State-year unwinding-intensity controls handle the confound from the post-FFCRA Medicaid restart.

C

What we didn’t find — and why.

Child coverage effects are smaller and noisier: a 0.63 pp uninsurance drop in ACS that doesn’t survive unwinding controls.

The sibling-FE estimate (Medicaid coverage, monthly) is +3.3 pp, p = 0.153. Most families simply enroll all eligible children together, leaving little within-family wedge for the policy to act on.

→

So what?

The economic value of multi-year continuous eligibility shows up where the policy discussion isn’t looking: in the parental labor market. Coverage stability for a child is an employment policy for the parent.
