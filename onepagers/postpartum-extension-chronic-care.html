From 60 days to 12 months: postpartum Medicaid extension and chronic-care continuity.

Medicaid pays for ~42% of U.S. births. The ARPA-enabled extension to 12 months postpartum is the largest maternal coverage policy change in a generation — and the chronic-care window is where it pays off.
Jonathan PalisocPh.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

States that adopted the 12-month postpartum Medicaid extension saw a 20–28 pp increase in continuous coverage through month 12, a +6.4 pp increase in any postpartum chronic-condition visit, and a ~14% reduction in postpartum ED use for chronic conditions. Effects concentrate in hypertension, diabetes, and mental health.

The policy wave.

The American Rescue Plan (March 2021) authorized states to extend pregnancy-related Medicaid from 60 days to 12 months postpartum; the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2023 made the option permanent. As of 2024, 48 states have adopted.

Why it matters.

  • One-third of pregnancy-related deaths occur 43 days to 1 year postpartum.
  • Medicaid pays for the plurality of U.S. births.
  • Coverage continuity is the precondition for any postpartum chronic-care intervention.
B

What we did.

Linked T-MSIS/TAF Medicaid claims with state policy adoption timing. Staggered DiD with CSA and DID2S; honest-DiD bounds on parallel trends.

C

Limitations.

  • Mortality outcomes underpowered at the state-year level.
  • Effect heterogeneity by state Medicaid management (FFS vs MCO) needs more years.
  • Selection into adoption timing partly correlated with maternal-health initiatives.
→

So what?

Twelve-month postpartum coverage delivers what proponents claimed: continuity, more chronic-care visits, fewer ED encounters. The remaining holdout states are leaving measurable maternal-health value on the table.
