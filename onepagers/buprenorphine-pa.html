Removing the gate: Medicaid prior authorization, buprenorphine, and overdose mortality.

Twelve states quietly dropped a paperwork rule on a lifesaving addiction medication. Prescribing jumped by tens of thousands of fills per quarter — with a plausible downstream signal in overdose deaths.
Jonathan Palisoc Ph.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

Buprenorphine roughly halves all-cause mortality in opioid use disorder — yet most Medicaid programs still gate it behind prior authorization (PA). Across the 12 states that dropped PA between 2015 and 2022, prescribing rose by an estimated 16,000+ fills per state-quarter. A back-of-the-envelope translation implies roughly 480 overdose deaths averted per year across the treated states.

But only about 11% of people with OUD ever receive medication. 53% of Medicaid enrollees who do face prior authorization — documentation of failed treatments, drug screens, dose caps, counseling contracts — gating a drug whose entire policy rationale is immediate office-based access.

A

Why this matters now.

  • Fentanyl drives ~75% of opioid deaths; the protective effect of medication has never been more consequential.
  • The 2023 elimination of the X-waiver added 11,600 new prescribers — but patient counts barely moved (Chua et al. 2024). Supply-side reform alone isn’t enough.
  • The HEAL Act and several state bills would prohibit PA for OUD medications. They need clean causal evidence.
B

What we did.

Linked Medicaid State Drug Utilization Data and CDC WONDER overdose mortality, 2011–2024, to a hand-built panel of state PA-removal events.

Used the Gardner (2022) DID2S and Dube et al. (2023) LP-DiD estimators — both unbiased under the heterogeneous treatment effects that break standard TWFE in staggered designs.

C

What’s new.

  • Doubles the treated-state count of the prior best study (12 vs. 6).
  • First multi-state design to follow PA removal all the way through to downstream overdose mortality.
  • Diagnoses why per-enrollee prescribing rates look noisier than total counts: denominator instability in Medicaid enrollment.
→

So what?

Prior authorization on a drug that halves mortality is the most expensive paperwork in American health policy. Removing it is cheap, fast, budget-neutral. The default should be no PA for buprenorphine.
