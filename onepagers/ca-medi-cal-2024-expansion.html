Policy One-Pager Medicaid Immigrant Coverage
Working Paper · 2026

Coverage without citizenship: California’s 2024 Medi-Cal adult expansion.

In January 2024, California became the first state to offer full-scope Medicaid coverage to every income-eligible adult, regardless of immigration status. One year in, the public data already shows it moving.
Jonathan Palisoc Ph.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

Among low-income noncitizens ages 26–49 — the cell directly opened by the expansion — California’s 2024 uninsurance rate was 26.8%, against a synthetic counterfactual of 34.3%. A −7.5 pp drop in uninsurance, mirrored by an +11.8 pp rise in Medicaid/means-tested coverage. The placebo rank stops short of a final causal stamp — but the direction and magnitude are exactly what the policy was built to produce.

03

Why the design choice matters.

Most surveys identify noncitizens, not undocumented residents — the population the policy actually targets. This paper makes a deliberate trade:

  • ACS noncitizens 26–49 at 1–200% FPL — a public-data proxy that’s available within a year and a half of implementation.
  • Donor pool screens out states with their own immigrant coverage policies to avoid contamination.
  • Ridge-augmented synthetic control corrects when California sits outside the donor convex hull.
A

Why this matters now.

  • Eleven states + DC now offer some form of state-funded immigrant coverage. California is the largest test case by an order of magnitude.
  • Several states are weighing rollbacks on fiscal grounds (notably Illinois, Minnesota). The first-stage evidence is now in.
  • Federal proposals would penalize states that cover undocumented residents. The size of the population at risk is no longer hypothetical.
B

The honest caveats.

  • ACS noncitizenship is a proxy, not a direct undocumented identifier.
  • The post-period is one year; pre-fit is strong but short pre-windows can produce fragile synthetic controls.
  • Placebo-in-space rank statistic is suggestive but not conclusive — this is an activation screen, not a final causal verdict.
C

What comes next.

  • Compare ASCM with SDID and generalized synthetic control.
  • Year-by-year audit of donor-state immigrant policy calendars.
  • Add utilization and access outcomes — primary-care visits, unmet need, financial hardship — if survey sources can support them.
→

So what?

Within twelve months, ACS already detects California’s expansion in the target population. State-funded coverage for noncitizens isn’t hypothetically effective — it’s visibly effective in public data within a year. That’s the right speed for policy decisions.
