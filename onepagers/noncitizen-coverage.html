Coverage by state choice: did state-funded Medicaid for undocumented adults work?

Sixteen states stepped past federal exclusions to extend coverage. California’s clean within-state design says yes — modestly. The national triple-difference is louder but messier.
Jonathan Palisoc Ph.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

Across 2018–2024 ACS data on 2.6 million low-income adults, California’s age-based phased rollout yields a clean coverage gain of 1.3–1.7 pp. Broad national triple-difference specs show +3.9 to +6.6 pp, but refined eligibility-restricted specs collapse to +0.4 pp and a fake-expansion placebo is highly significant. Evidence hierarchy: trust California; treat national as supporting.

The California experiment.

Medi-Cal phased eligibility to adults 50+ in May 2022, then 26–49 in January 2024. The window between gives a clean within-state DiD: older noncitizens covered, younger ones not, all else identical.

A

Why it matters now.

  • ~10.5–11 million undocumented residents; ~50% of adults uninsured.
  • Curtailment is already underway — the question shifts from “did it work?” to “what will reversal cost?”
  • Uncompensated care, ED reliance, and chronic-disease management all flow through this coverage margin.
B

What we did.

Two identification strategies on 2018–2024 ACS data (N = 2.6M below 200% FPL):

  1. California age-based DiD — within-state, eliminates cross-state confounders.
  2. National triple-difference — citizen vs. noncitizen × expanding vs. non-expanding × pre/post.

Robustness via Rambachan–Roth HonestDiD, cluster-robust SEs (CRV1, 51 clusters), and a trend-break design.

C

Evidence hierarchy.

  • Anchor: California within-state design (1.3–1.7 pp).
  • Supportive but not decisive: national triple-difference (3.9–6.6 pp) — pre-trends flagged.
  • Effects concentrated among low-income and Hispanic noncitizens, consistent with Medicaid targeting.
→

So what?

These programs produced genuine but modest coverage gains — better characterized by California (1.5 pp) than by the louder national headline. Recent curtailments now risk reversing those gains in an enforcement-heightened environment.
