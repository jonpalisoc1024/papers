Squeezing the spread: do state PBM transparency laws actually lower Medicaid drug spend?

A wave of states banned spread pricing or moved to pass-through MCO pharmacy contracts. The visible savings show up where you’d expect — and where you wouldn’t.
Jonathan PalisocPh.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

Across the 2018–2024 wave of state pharmacy-benefit reforms, transparent (pass-through) PBM contracts reduce reported per-script Medicaid spend by an estimated 4–7%. The standalone effect of a “spread ban” statute — absent a pass-through procurement — is statistically indistinguishable from zero.

Three levers, often conflated.

  • Spread-ban statute — PBM cannot bill the plan more than it pays the pharmacy.
  • Pass-through procurement — the state contracts a PBM at a fixed admin fee.
  • Pharmacy carve-out — state administers fee-for-service directly.

The press release usually says “banned spread pricing.” The contract that actually delivers savings is often a pass-through procurement signed quarters later.

What we don’t see.

Total Medicaid pharmacy spend does not move 1:1 with per-script spend — rebate timing, utilization rebound, and 340B substitution all blunt the headline. Honest read: procurement reform is a unit-price lever, not a budget lever.

A

Why it matters.

  • Medicaid drug spend grew 72% FY17–FY23; pharmacy is a top fiscal pressure point.
  • State PBM reform is bipartisan and active — ~20 states moved in 7 years.
  • The wrong instrument (spread ban only) is being scored as if it equals the right one (procurement reform).
B

What we did.

Built a state-quarter panel from CMS State Drug Utilization Data and the CMS-64; coded each state’s spread-ban statute, pass-through procurement, and carve-out separately.

Staggered DiD with heterogeneity-robust estimators (DID2S, CSA); honest sensitivity on parallel trends.

C

Caveats.

  • SDUD timing lag — 2024 reforms only partially observed.
  • Rebate dollars are confidential; visible spend understates true net.
  • Effects vary with MCO market structure.
→

So what?

A spread-ban headline isn’t a budget plan. Real Medicaid pharmacy savings come from procurement architecture; legislatures should score the contract, not the statute.
