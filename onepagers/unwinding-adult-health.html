What the unwinding did to adult health: utilization, chronic care, and access.

25 million people lost Medicaid in the 2023–24 unwinding. The downstream health-care signal is now in the data — chronic-condition visits down, ED reliance up, gaps concentrated where you’d expect.
Jonathan PalisocPh.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

Across 2023–24, every 10-pp rise in a state’s procedural-disenrollment rate is associated with a −3.1% change in adult primary-care visits, a +2.4% change in ED visits for ambulatory-care-sensitive conditions, and a −5.8% change in chronic-medication adherence among formerly-enrolled adults. Effects concentrate in low-renewal-IT states.

Heterogeneity.

Concentrates among chronic-disease cohorts (HTN, DM, asthma, depression) and young adults in non-expansion states. Coverage transitions to marketplace and ESI partially offset effects in expansion states.

A

Why it matters.

  • Largest single coverage disruption in Medicaid history.
  • The downstream cost of paperwork churn lands on EDs and chronic-care management.
  • Eligibility reform proposals (work requirements, more frequent renewals) replicate the mechanism.
B

What we did.

State-month panel 2019–2024 of adult Medicaid claims; IV using the pre-pandemic auto-renewal tier (same instrument as Administrative Wedge). Honest-DiD bounds, state-clustered SEs.

C

Caveats.

  • Claims lag for late-2024 outcomes.
  • Some transitions captured by marketplace data; some are uninsurance.
  • Effects likely larger for the most chronically ill subgroup than the average.
→

So what?

Procedural churn isn’t neutral to health. The unwinding raised acute-care use and lowered primary care, especially in low-IT states — exactly the pattern the renewal-rule rewrite should be sized to prevent.
