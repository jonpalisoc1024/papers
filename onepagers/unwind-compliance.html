Federal compliance pauses during the unwinding: what worked, what didn’t.

CMS issued mid-unwinding compliance actions against 30 states. Pauses on procedural terminations reinstated coverage for ~500K people — with uneven follow-through.
Jonathan PalisocPh.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

The CMS “August 30 letter” (2023) and the December 2023 enforcement framework required 30 states to pause procedural disenrollments while fixing auto-renewal logic. Pauses reinstated ~497,000 enrollees nationwide. Procedural rates fell 11–14 pp in the targeted month but rebounded partially after the pause lifted.

The compliance lever.

  • August 2023 CMS letter to 30 states with auto-renewal failures.
  • Required pause on procedural terminations until logic fixed.
  • Reinstatement — not reapplication — was the remedy.

03

State heterogeneity.

States with deeper auto-renewal investments before the pandemic completed remediation faster and didn’t recur. States with weaker IT infrastructure entered repeated compliance cycles. The same wedge from the Administrative Wedge paper.

A

Why it matters.

  • The unwinding is the largest disenrollment in Medicaid history.
  • CMS compliance actions are one of the few real federal levers mid-event.
  • The next renewal-rule rewrite needs evidence on what compliance enforcement actually achieved.
B

What we did.

Synthesized CMS publicly-issued compliance letters, KFF state tracker data, and state-month CMS PI dashboards. Event-study DiD around pause dates; differentiate states by auto-renewal investment tier.

C

Caveats.

  • Reinstatement vs. continuous coverage are not equivalent — gaps remain in claims data.
  • Some pauses overlap with other state-level pauses.
  • Selection of which states CMS sanctioned is itself non-random.
→

So what?

Federal mid-event compliance enforcement works — while the pause holds. Durable improvements require the renewal-IT investments that determine who churns in the first place.
