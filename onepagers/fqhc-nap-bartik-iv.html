Working Paper · 2026

Health centers as enrollment infrastructure: FQHC capacity and county uninsurance.

FQHCs aren’t just clinics — they’re where eligible patients get screened, enrolled, and converted to Medicaid. New shift-share evidence puts a number on the coverage spillover.
Jonathan Palisoc Ph.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

An additional grant-driven FQHC site per 10,000 county residents is associated with a 3.90-percentage-point reduction in under-65 uninsurance (composite shift-share IV, post-2016). A tighter HPSA/MUA-only specification yields a smaller, borderline 1.33 pp. Read the result as a bundled-capacity effect — sites, staff, outreach, eligibility assistance moving together — not a per-clinic constant.

−3.90 pp
Under-65 uninsurance, per FQHC site / 10k. Bartik IV · 95% CI −6.80 to −0.99
01

The hidden role of an FQHC.

Federally Qualified Health Centers serve ~31 million patients a year, half of them on Medicaid. Their Section 330 grants pay for more than exam rooms:

  • Outreach staff who find eligible-uninsured residents.
  • Eligibility counselors and certified application counselors.
  • Case managers who shepherd patients through paperwork.
  • A revenue model that only works if walk-ins become covered patients ($1,418 Medicaid PPS vs. ~$0 from the uninsured).
02

The Bartik instrument.

Identification: interact a county’s baseline exposure to HRSA allocation criteria (HPSA, MUA, baseline poverty, baseline uninsurance) with the national time series of Section 330 grant funding 1999–2026.

The shifts come from federal appropriations cycles — Bush HCI, ARRA, the ACA Community Health Center Fund, and the post-2016 plateau — not from county-level demand.

Diagnostics: Borusyak–Hull–Jaravel shift-exogeneity · GPSS Rotemberg weights · AKM exposure-robust SEs
03

Two estimates, two stories.

The honest read: a meaningful but local, design-dependent association — not a universal per-clinic effect.

A

Why it matters.

  • The Community Health Center Fund cycles through short reauthorizations; every cliff debate needs causal numbers.
  • FQHCs are the largest federally-supported primary-care safety net in the U.S.
  • The coverage channel runs through enrollment infrastructure, not provider supply — a mechanism rarely measured.
B

What we did.

Built a county-year panel linking HRSA UDS/H80 grant ledgers, FQHC sites, baseline need, and Census SAHIE under-65 uninsurance — bridging the 2013–15 USAspending “cliff” that breaks most prior pipelines.

Bartik 2SLS with county and fiscal-year fixed effects; exposure-robust inference via Adão–Kolesár–Morales.

C

Scope & caveats.

  • Effect strongest in the post-2016 plateau; pre-2016 weakly identified.
  • A Ryan White placebo using shared composite shares is non-null — outcome choice matters.
  • SAHIE vs. ACS as outcome shifts magnitude; HPSA/MUA-only is the conservative anchor.
→

So what?

Section 330 funding doesn’t just buy primary care — it buys coverage uptake. CBO scores of FQHC funding should price in the enrollment-spillover channel, and reauthorization cliffs should be evaluated against the coverage losses they risk.
