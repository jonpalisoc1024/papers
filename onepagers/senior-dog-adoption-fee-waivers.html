Free is not the cheapest: fee waivers and senior dog adoption.

A randomized fee-waiver experiment across municipal shelters changes who walks in — not always who walks out with a senior dog. The free price has well-documented adoption pulls and well-documented compositional risks.
Jonathan PalisocPh.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

In a multi-shelter randomized experiment, waiving the adoption fee for senior dogs (≥7 years) increased weekly senior adoptions by +62% over a control fee. Six-month return-to-shelter rates were statistically indistinguishable across arms (3.1% vs. 3.7%). The familiar concern that “free pets are returned” was not borne out at this margin.

The design.

Who came in.

Waivers shifted the adopter pool toward first-time and lower-income households — previously priced out. Application-to-adoption conversion rose at the half-fee arm and was highest at the full waiver. No detectable rise in surrender rates of other pets in adopter households.

A

Why it matters.

  • Senior dogs have the longest length-of-stay in U.S. shelters.
  • Shelters routinely block fee waivers on a quality-of-adopter concern that the data don’t support at this margin.
  • The fee is a screening tool — and the wrong one to screen on.
B

What we did.

Pre-registered cluster-randomized field experiment. Outcomes: weekly adoptions, return-to-shelter (30/90/180/365 day), surrender of other household pets, applicant counseling pass-through.

C

Caveats.

  • Generalizes to senior dogs in municipal shelters with light counseling protocols.
  • Sponsored-fee ≠ free-to-rescue: shelter still covers vet, microchip, spay/neuter costs.
  • Adopter follow-up incomplete at the long tail.
→

So what?

The senior-dog fee waiver is a tested, near-costless lever to reduce length-of-stay without raising returns. The bottleneck isn’t price — it’s the misconception that price is a screen worth keeping.
