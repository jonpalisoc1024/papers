Policy One-Pager Medicaid Home Care
Working Paper · 2026

Watching the workers: electronic visit verification and the Medicaid home-care market.

Congress required GPS tracking of every Medicaid home-care visit and projected $290 million in fraud savings. Five years in, the aggregate claims data shows… almost nothing.
Jonathan Palisoc Ph.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

The 21st Century Cures Act mandated Electronic Visit Verification (EVV) for all Medicaid personal-care services by 2020 and home health by 2023 — with CBO projecting $290 million in federal savings. The first causal evaluation finds no statistically significant spending effect on any margin: price, utilization, providers, or beneficiaries. The CBO savings cannot be ruled out — aggregate data is roughly 200× too noisy to detect them.

A null result with a power asterisk.

The CBO projected total computable savings of about $0.25M per state-quarter. The minimum effect this design can detect is $49.76M per state-quarter.

The aggregate-data signal-to-noise ratio is 200× too poor to confirm or refute the CBO. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

03

What we can rule out — and can’t.

  • Can rule out: large provider exits. Provider counts barely moved (−1.2, p=0.79).
  • Can rule out: a transition shock to beneficiary access.
  • Can’t rule out: small per-claim fraud-deterrence savings of the CBO’s magnitude.
  • Can’t rule out: savings offset by improved documentation of legitimate visits previously under-billed.
A

Why this matters now.

  • Medicaid home and community-based services exceed $100 billion / year; personal-care attendant fraud has run 34–43% of all Medicaid Fraud Control Unit convictions.
  • EVV is now operational in all 51 jurisdictions for PCS and 44 for home health.
  • Several federal proposals would expand the EVV framework to other Medicaid services. Knowing whether the existing rollout paid off — or just imposed compliance cost — is the threshold question.
B

What we did.

Hand-coded EVV implementation timing for all 51 jurisdictions; merged with T-MSIS Medicaid claims (quarterly, provider-procedure level) and CMS Financial Management Reports as secondary validation.

Three estimators — TWFE, Gardner DID2S, triple-difference exploiting the three-year gap between PCS and HHCS deadlines — all converge on the same precisely-null result.

C

Honest caveats.

  • T-MSIS has known managed-care quality issues — my estimates are an intent-to-treat lower bound.
  • HHCS pre-trends are rejected (p=0.0002) — the home-health result is fragile; PCS is clean.
  • Formal compliance lags operational implementation; treatment effects could be diluted.
→

So what?

EVV produced no detectable aggregate savings — but the design isn’t powered to find the CBO’s number. The right policy update isn’t “EVV failed.” It’s: any future compliance mandate should ship with a measurement plan that can actually see the savings it promises.
