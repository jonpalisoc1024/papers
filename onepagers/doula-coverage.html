Cradle equity: state Medicaid doula coverage and the populations it was built for.

Twenty-eight states now cover doula services under Medicaid. Pooled outcomes look flat. Look inside the average, and the policy is doing exactly what it was designed to do.
Jonathan Palisoc Ph.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

Pooled state-year effects are statistically indistinguishable from zero. That isn’t the story. Stratified by race, Medicaid doula coverage produces meaningful reductions in NH Black preterm birth (−2.86 pp) and low birthweight (−3.37 pp), and large gains in NH AIAN prenatal-care access. This is a distributional intervention — it moves outcomes for the populations facing the largest baseline disparities, which is what equity policy is supposed to do.

Robustness, told honestly.

  • Callaway–Sant’Anna preferred over TWFE: Goodman–Bacon shows 28% of TWFE weight comes from problematic already-treated comparisons.
  • Survives drop-OR/MN, drop-2024, postpartum-overlap drops, HonestDiD smoothness bounds.
  • None of the 10 nominally significant cells survive Bonferroni across 50 tests — defended on pre-specification grounds, not data-mining.
A

Why this matters.

  • The US has a maternal mortality rate 2–3× peer high-income countries; disparities have been stable for decades.
  • MACPAC’s 2023 report explicitly called for population-level evaluation of doula benefits. This paper is the first.
  • States deciding whether to adopt, expand, or sustain doula benefits have been relying on RCTs of in-person labor support, not state-program evidence.
B

What’s new here.

  • First quasi-experimental, population-level evaluation of state Medicaid doula coverage.
  • First analysis to show the distributional pattern: equity-targeted policies will look null in pooled means.
  • Extends the outcome set into prenatal-care access, where the NH AIAN gains are largest.
C

Limits.

  • State-aggregate, not individual-level outcomes.
  • Six-year analytic window; some adopting cohorts have very short post-periods.
  • Some states co-timed postpartum extension; effects unstack only partly.
→

So what?

Doula coverage is doing what advocates promised — narrowing the disparities at the heart of the maternal-health crisis. Evaluations that look only at pooled means will conclude the policy doesn’t work. They’ll be wrong.
