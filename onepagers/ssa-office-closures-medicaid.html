When the SSA office closes: spillover effects on Medicaid enrollment.

SSA field-office closures concentrate the burden of in-person verification on the disabled and dual-eligible. They reduce SSI awards — and that reduction propagates into Medicaid enrollment churn.
Jonathan PalisocPh.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

Counties experiencing a SSA field-office closure see SSI awards fall by ~11% in the two years post-closure and dual-eligible Medicaid enrollment fall by an estimated 3–5%. Effects concentrate among rural counties >25 miles to the nearest remaining office.

The closure pattern.

Since 2010, SSA has closed 67 field offices and consolidated dozens of hearing offices. Closures correlate with rural exposure and aging infrastructure, not population shifts.

Who absorbs the burden.

Drop concentrates among rural and older applicants; the share of awards going to applicants with a household member to assist is unchanged, but the absolute count of awards in single-applicant households falls more than 2× the average.

A

Why it matters.

  • SSI eligibility is the on-ramp to Medicaid in 33 §1634 states.
  • Field-office consolidation is an administrative-burden policy by another name.
  • The administrative spillover into Medicaid is invisible in SSA’s closure scoring.
B

What we did.

County-quarter panel 2008–2024 linking SSA field-office closures, SSI determinations, and Medicaid enrollment (T-MSIS). Event-study DiD; instrumented closures via predetermined building-age + lease-expiry covariates.

C

Caveats.

  • Online and phone substitution partly offsets closures — not for everyone.
  • §1634 vs SSI-criteria states experience different Medicaid pass-throughs.
  • Effects persist 3+ years; longer-run TBD.
→

So what?

SSA office closures are not just an SSA decision — they push thousands of people out of Medicaid in §1634 states. Closure cost-benefit should price the downstream Medicaid effect.
