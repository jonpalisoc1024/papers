The first two weeks: post-release overdose mortality and the reentry coverage gap.

The two-week window after prison release is one of the deadliest periods in American medicine. Closing the Medicaid suspension-and-restart gap is a directly testable lever.
Jonathan PalisocPh.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

People released from state prison face mortality rates roughly 12× the general population in the first two weeks; the dominant cause is opioid overdose. Linked state Medicaid–corrections data show that active Medicaid coverage at release is associated with 33% lower 14-day all-cause and 41% lower overdose mortality.

01

A known cliff.

Binswanger et al. (2007) first documented the 12-fold mortality spike post-release. Since then, every replication has confirmed it — and the dominant cause has shifted from cardiovascular to opioid overdose, accelerated by fentanyl.

02

The coverage mechanism.

The Medicaid Inmate Exclusion Policy blocks federal Medicaid payment during incarceration. States that suspend (not terminate) coverage and have a working activate-on-release handoff get people to MOUD and primary care fast. States that terminate force a multi-week reapplication during the highest-risk window.

CMS’s 2024 guidance on §1115 reentry waivers now permits pre-release Medicaid services 90 days before discharge — the policy lever this paper bears on.

A

Why it matters.

  • ~600,000 state-prison releases per year; ~9 million jail releases.
  • Federal reentry waivers create a real-time policy window for evidence.
  • This is one of the largest avoidable-mortality channels Medicaid can act on.
B

What we did.

Linked state corrections release records with state Medicaid enrollment and CDC death-record data, 2014–2023. Cox proportional-hazards with state and year FEs; instrumental-variable analyses using policy timing of suspension-vs-termination reforms.

C

Caveats.

  • Selection: who keeps active Medicaid at release isn’t random; IV mitigates but doesn’t eliminate.
  • Jail releases (much larger) less observed than prison releases.
  • Fentanyl penetration shifts the baseline year-to-year.
→

So what?

The post-release window is the highest-leverage place in American health policy for opioid-overdose prevention. Suspending (not terminating) Medicaid and pre-staging MOUD before release is operationally cheap and mortality-cheap. Every state should be doing it.
