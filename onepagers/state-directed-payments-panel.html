State directed payments: a $100B+ parallel finance system inside Medicaid managed care.

SDPs let states direct MCO payments to specific providers above the negotiated rate. They’ve grown from a niche tool to a structural feature — with no panel evidence on their effects.
Jonathan PalisocPh.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

State-directed payments grew from ~$20B (FY18) to over $110B (FY24); 40+ states now use them. SDPs concentrate in hospitals and academic centers, raise Medicaid hospital revenue per discharge by an estimated 11–19%, and have no detectable association with quality outcomes at the state-year level. Selection of recipients is itself the policy.

What an SDP is.

  • State directs an MCO to pay specific providers a specific amount above the negotiated rate.
  • Three flavors: uniform dollar/percentage increase, minimum fee schedule, value-based pass-through.
  • Funded jointly with federal match; sourced via state appropriations or provider taxes.
02

The growth.

Total approved SDP dollars roughly 5.5× from FY18 to FY24. Hospital SDPs are the dominant share; nursing home and physician SDPs are smaller and more recent.

What we can’t yet see.

Public CMS-64 and SDP approval letters don’t resolve which providers within a state get how much. Without that, the within-state distributional question — the policy’s real point — is hidden.

A

Why it matters.

  • SDPs are now the largest discretionary lever in Medicaid finance.
  • They sit outside the rate-cell architecture — opaque to standard analyses.
  • The proposed federal payment rule (CMS-2439-F) constrains but doesn’t eliminate them.
B

What we did.

Built a state-year panel of approved SDPs from CMS preprint letters, MACPAC annual reports, and OACT. Linked to hospital cost reports and CMS quality compendia. Cohort-staggered DiD on hospital revenue per Medicaid discharge.

C

Caveats.

  • Approved dollars overstate paid dollars in some years.
  • Provider-level allocation isn’t public — state-year results are an aggregate of within-state heterogeneity.
  • 2024–25 federal rule changes will shift the trajectory.
→

So what?

Medicaid finance has quietly grown a $110B parallel system that operates outside the rate-cell architecture. The next round of federal rulemaking will be far better informed if it forces provider-level transparency on SDP allocation.
