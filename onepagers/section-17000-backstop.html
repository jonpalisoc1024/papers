Working Paper · 2026

California’s last-resort: §17000 as the indigent-care backstop after Medi-Cal contracts.

Welfare and Institutions Code §17000 obliges counties to provide care to indigent residents not covered by other programs. As Medi-Cal narrows around immigration and reentry, the §17000 backstop reawakens.
Jonathan PalisocPh.D. Candidate, Health Services Org. & Policy
University of Michigan
The bottom line

The 2026 Medi-Cal enrollment freeze for newly arriving undocumented adults shifts an estimated 180,000–300,000 Californians annually to county §17000 responsibility within 24 months. County indigent-care budgets — eroded since 2014 — are unprepared. The legal duty remains; the operating capacity does not.

01

What §17000 is.

  • 1933 statute — counties “shall relieve and support” indigent residents.
  • Standard of care set by Hunt v. Superior Court (1999) and a string of trial-court decisions.
  • Capacity built around the Medically Indigent Adult population; eroded after 2014 Medi-Cal expansion.
02

What changed in 2026.

The Medi-Cal enrollment freeze for newly arriving undocumented adults (Jan 2026) and the partial Illinois-style retrenchment of state-funded adult coverage push uninsured population back to the county tier — the original §17000 floor.

Why it matters.

  • Medi-Cal contractions don’t eliminate the legal duty — they relocate it.
  • County capacity is uneven: a handful of counties run real programs; most don’t.
  • The litigation exposure is real and historical.
B

What we did.

Synthesized statutory history, the post-Hunt case law, county MOE reports, CHCF coverage data, and recent §1115 retrenchments. Microsimulated the post-freeze population shift using ACS + KFF undocumented estimates.

C

Caveats.

  • Migration response to retrenchment unknown.
  • County maintenance-of-effort accounting varies; figures bounded.
  • Hospital-district vs. county-clinic models react differently.
→

So what?

Reversing state-funded coverage doesn’t erase the care need — it reawakens a 1933 county obligation that was never repealed. The bill comes in two forms: uncompensated care and litigation. The state should plan for both.
